Parliamentary elections were held in Finland <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Finland> on 2 April 2023 to elect members of the Parliament of
Finland <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Finland>.

Following the 2019 election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2019_Finnish_parliamentary_election>, the Social Democratic Party
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Finland>
formed a governing coalition <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Rinne_Cabinet> with the Centre Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Centre_Party_(Finland)>, Greens <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Green_League>, Left Alliance <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Left_Alliance_(Finland)>, and the Swedish People's Party <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_People%27s_Party_of_Finland>, with Antti
Rinne <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antti_Rinne> serving as the prime
minister of Finland <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Prime_minister_of_Finland>. Later that year, Rinne was involved in a
political scandal <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2019_Finnish_postal_strike> regarding the Finnish postal service
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Posti_Group>, after which he resigned and
was succeeded by Sanna Marin <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Sanna_Marin>. Marin's government <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Marin_Cabinet> focused on issues regarding climate change, while her
government also faced the COVID-19 pandemic <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/COVID-19_pandemic> and the Russian invasion of Ukraine <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine>. The invasion
prompted her to announce that Finland would submit its candidacy to join
NATO <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO>, which it did in May 2022.

Issues regarding the economy, the budget deficit, climate change, and
education were discussed during the campaign period. The opposition
parties NCP and Finns election programs called for budget cuts and
austerity to balance the budget deficits, while the SDP's election
program focused on economic growth through investments in employment and
education to balance the budget. Marin stated her support for more
education and public health spending, while the National Coalition
called for balancing the economy, reducing spending on unemployment and
housing benefits, and constructing more nuclear plants. The Finns
campaigned on an anti-immigration <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-
immigration> and anti-European Union <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Euroscepticism> platform, while the Greens focused on issues regarding
mental health and universal basic income <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Universal_basic_income>. The Centre Party campaigned on regional
politics <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regionalism_(politics)>.

The National Coalition Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
National_Coalition_Party> won the election with 20.8% of the national
popular vote and winning 48 seats in parliament, the third-highest
result in the party's history. The party went on to form a coalition
with the Finns <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finns_Party>, Swedish
People's Party, and the Christian Democrats <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Christian_Democrats_(Finland)>, with Petteri Orpo <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petteri_Orpo> as prime minister. For the first
time in Finland's history, the Centre Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Centre_Party_(Finland)> did not win a plurality of votes in any
regional voting districts.^[1] <#cite_note-1> Five government parties
and four opposition parties finished with parliamentary seats split at
100–100. This was the first time since 1983 <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/1983_Finnish_parliamentary_election> that the top-three finishing
parties by results did not lose share of votes by percentage points.


    Background[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Finnish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=1>]

Antti Rinne giving a speech in the European Parliament in July 2019
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
File:Finnish_Council_Presidency_priorities_debated_in_plenary_(48308299127)_(cropped).jpg>
Antti Rinne resigned as prime minister following the postal service
scandal in December 2019.

The previous parliamentary election, held in April 2019, saw the Social
Democratic Party place first, winning 17.7% of the popular vote,
followed by the Finns Party and the National Coalition Party, who won
17.5% and 17% of the popular vote, respectively.^[2] <#cite_note-2> ^[3]
<#cite_note-3> The Centre Party of the outgoing prime minister Juha
Sipilä <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juha_Sipil%C3%A4> placed fourth,
winning 13.8% of the popular vote, while the Green League and the Left
Alliance received more votes than in the preceding election.^[4]
<#cite_note-:17-4> After several months of negotiations, the SDP led by
Rinne formed a government in June 2019, bringing the Centre Party, Green
League, Left Alliance, and the Swedish People's Party into the
government.^[5] <#cite_note-:18-5> ^[6] <#cite_note-:19-6> ^[7]
<#cite_note-:20-7>

In late 2019, a political scandal regarding the Finnish postal service
broke out.^[8] <#cite_note-:21-8> Sirpa Paatero <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirpa_Paatero>, the minister of local government
and ownership steering <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Minister_of_Local_Government_and_Ownership_Steering_(Finland)>, was
accused of being aware of and hiding from members of the Parliament of
Finland <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_of_Finland> the postal
service's project to change the status of a large number of its workers
to a lower-paying contract.^[9] <#cite_note-:0-9> Paatero resigned in
November 2019,^[10] <#cite_note-:22-10> after which Rinne was also
accused of knowing the postal service plan.^[9] <#cite_note-:0-9> The
Centre Party then left the government, which led Rinne to announce his
resignation as prime minister and leader of the SDP in December
2019.^[11] <#cite_note-:23-11> ^[12] <#cite_note-:24-12> He was
succeeded by Sanna Marin <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Sanna_Marin>.^[13] <#cite_note-:25-13> ^[14] <#cite_note-:26-14> Marin
became the world's youngest serving prime minister after being sworn
into office in December 2019.^[15] <#cite_note-:27-15>

The Marin Cabinet <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marin_Cabinet>
announced they would combat climate change, protect the environment, and
reduce social and economic inequalities.^[16] <#cite_note-:1-16> Her
government also adopted a program that would aim to make Finland carbon-
neutral by 2035 by reducing greenhouse gas emissions and increasing the
production of renewable energy.^[16] <#cite_note-:1-16> Following the
outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic in Finland <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/COVID-19_pandemic_in_Finland>, Marin's management of the pandemic
benefited her party, causing her to rise in popularity in opinion polls,
with her personal favourable opinion record rate reaching 85%.^[17]
<#cite_note-:28-17> ^[18] <#cite_note-:29-18> ^[19] <#cite_note-:30-19>
The SDP was surpassed by the Finns Party in opinion polls after April
2021, with the National Coalition Party becoming the most popular by
July 2021.^[a] <#cite_note-25> ^[25] <#cite_note-:31-26> ^[26]
<#cite_note-:32-27>

Following the beginning of the Russian invasion of Ukraine <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine>, Marin took a firm
line against Russia and Vladimir Putin <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Vladimir_Putin>, breaking Finland's historical military neutrality
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_neutrality> by announcing
Finland's NATO candidacy <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Finland%27s_NATO_candidancy>.^[27] <#cite_note-:33-28> ^[28]
<#cite_note-:34-29> ^[29] <#cite_note-:35-30> Finland officially
submitted its candidacy in May 2022.^[30] <#cite_note-:36-31> Its path
to join NATO was formalized in March 2023, when Recep Tayyip Erdoğan
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recep_Tayyip_Erdo%C4%9Fan>, the president
of Turkey, formally signed and approved the decision to ratify Finland's
NATO membership.^[31] <#cite_note-:37-32> ^[32] <#cite_note-:38-33>
Turkey was the last NATO member to ratify the decision.^[33]
<#cite_note-:39-34>


    Electoral system[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Finnish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=2>]

The 200 members of the Parliament of Finland <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Parliament_of_Finland> (/Eduskunta/, /Riksdagen/) were elected
using open list <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_list>, proportional
representation <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Proportional_representation> in 13 multi-member electoral districts
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_districts_of_Finland>, with
seats allocated according to the D'Hondt method <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Hondt_method>.^[34] <#cite_note-:2-35> The
number of elected representatives is proportional to the population in
the district six months prior to the elections. Åland <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%85land> has a single-member electoral district
and its own party system.^[34] <#cite_note-:2-35> ^[35] <#cite_note-36>
Every citizen who is at least 18 years old has the right to vote.^[36]
<#cite_note-37> Voters also had the option to vote during the advance
voting period or on election day.^[37] <#cite_note-:3-38> Advance voting
took place from March 22 to 28 March 2023.^[38] <#cite_note-39> ^[39]
<#cite_note-:4-40>

      Issues[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2023_Finnish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=6>]

An image of campaign posters in Helsinki in March 2023 <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
File:Eduskuntavaalit_2023_ehdokasjulisteita_L%C3%A4nsilinkki,_Helsinki.jpg>
Campaign posters in Helsinki <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helsinki> in
March 2023

Yle <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yle> cited government borrowing,
sustainability of public finances, climate change, and educational
decline as main issues during the electoral campaign.^[43]
<#cite_note-50> Paul Kirby of BBC News <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
BBC_News> noted that the Russian invasion of Ukraine has had "little
campaign impact", and added that the campaign period was instead
concentrated on issues regarding the economy.^[44] <#cite_note-:6-51>
Deutsche Welle <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deutsche_Welle> cited a
shortage of workers as a key issue during the campaign period, while the
Associated Press <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_Press>
mentioned that issues regarding the economy, climate change, education,
and social benefits were discussed during the campaign.^[45]
<#cite_note-52> ^[46] <#cite_note-:10-53> Markku Jokisipilä, an
assistant professor at the University of Turku <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Turku>, said that in the debates,
Marin has "stood out vigorously". He added that the Finns Party had the
strongest social media presence.^[47] <#cite_note-54> Teivo Teivainen
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teivo_Teivainen>, a professor at the
University of Helsinki <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
University_of_Helsinki>, said that opponents of the SDP's main problem
is public spending.^[48] <#cite_note-:9-55> News agencies described the
election as a close race between the SDP, National Coalition Party, and
the Finns Party.^[44] <#cite_note-:6-51> ^[46] <#cite_note-:10-53> ^[49]
<#cite_note-56> ^[50] <#cite_note-:13-57> Jenni Karimäki, a political
historian who works at the University of Helsinki, said that "nobody can
know what order the three leading parties will finish in on
Sunday."^[51] <#cite_note-:11-58>

The Finns Party, which campaigned on an anti-immigration and anti-
European Union <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union> (EU)
platform,^[46] <#cite_note-:10-53> cited their priority to cut
immigration from non-EU countries. Petteri Orpo <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petteri_Orpo>, the leader of the National
Coalition Party, campaigned on reducing spending on unemployment and
housing benefits.^[52] <#cite_note-:7-59> While campaigning, Orpo stated
his support for economic growth, balancing the public economy, "building
up NATO-Finland", and constructing more nuclear plants.^[46]
<#cite_note-:10-53> ^[53] <#cite_note-:8-60> Riikka Purra <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riikka_Purra>, the leader of the Finns Party,
called for austerity <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austerity>, and said
that "we also want to tighten up our attitude towards the European
Union."^[53] <#cite_note-:8-60> She added that the Finns Party would
delay the carbon neutrality target that was introduced by Marin's
government.^[48] <#cite_note-:9-55> ^[54] <#cite_note-:12-61> The
National Coalition Party agreed with Marin's position on carbon
neutrality.^[46] <#cite_note-:10-53> Marin remained critical of right-
wing parties, saying that they offered "an alternative that makes life
miserable for all of us, cuts services, cuts livelihoods for the
poorest"; she had also stated her support for education and public
health spending, saying that it would help Finland to avoid
borrowing.^[51] <#cite_note-:11-58> ^[54] <#cite_note-:12-61> The Green
League's campaign was focused on mental health services and universal
basic income <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_basic_income>. The
Centre Party campaigned on regional politics <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Regional_politics>.^[50] <#cite_note-:13-57>

    Aftermath[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Finnish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=21>]


      Government formation[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2023_Finnish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=22>]

Before the election, the Social Democratic Party <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Finland>, Green League
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_League>, and the Left Alliance
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_Alliance_(Finland)> ruled out
joining a coalition that involved the Finns Party <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finns_Party>. The Swedish People's Party <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_People%27s_Party_of_Finland> similarly
stated that they were unlikely to align with the Finns but did not rule
the possibility out completely. According to Swedish People's Party
leader Anna-Maja Henriksson <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anna-
Maja_Henriksson>, her party wants to see how government talks will go
before making a final decision.^[92] <#cite_note-coalition-99> The Left
Alliance also stated that they were not ready to enter a coalition that
involved the National Coalition Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
National_Coalition_Party>.^[92] <#cite_note-coalition-99> At the
beginning of March 2023, Centre Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Centre_Party_(Finland)> leader Annika Saarikko <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annika_Saarikko> stated that even though she was
not ruling out a coalition with any party, she was not willing to
continue remaining in the incumbent coalition government due to policy
disputes with other coalition parties, especially the Green League.^[92]
<#cite_note-coalition-99> ^[93] <#cite_note-coalition2-100> National
Coalition Party leader Petteri Orpo <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Petteri_Orpo> said he was keeping his options open. News agencies
predicted that if his party was the largest, he would need to team up
with the Finns Party or the Social Democratic Party, along with smaller
parties, to get a majority.^[93] <#cite_note-coalition2-100>

After the immediate results of the election, the incumbent coalition did
not have an absolute majority in parliament. The results showed that
Orpo had to face difficult options, as his party differed from the Finns
on immigration, climate, and EU membership status. Additionally, forming
a coalition with them was said to be difficult because some parties had
already ruled out a coalition with the Finns. On the other hand, he
vowed to replace the economic policy of the Social Democrats. At that
point, it was widely anticipated that the Centre Party, Green League,
and Left Alliance would prefer to go into opposition rather than join a
new coalition. This left the Swedish People's Party and Christian
Democrats <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Democrats_(Finland)>,
which held their ground, as likely coalition partners.^[94]
<#cite_note-:14-101> ^[95] <#cite_note-coalition3-102> ^[96]
<#cite_note-coalition4-103> ^[97] <#cite_note-coalition5-104> The media
reported that Orpo was leaning towards talks with the Finns Party first,
but also that he was sending out a questionnaire to each of the other
parties to assess their positions on various topics.^[98] <#cite_note-:
15-105> ^[99] <#cite_note-:16-106> ^[95] <#cite_note-coalition3-102>

On 4 April 2023, leader of the Social Democratic Party Sanna Marin
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanna_Marin> announced that she resigned
as chairwoman of her party, but remained a lawmaker in parliament.^[100]
<#cite_note-coalition6-107> Centre Party leader Annika Saarikko
confirmed that the Centre Party's place was in the opposition following
the party's election losses. Green League party leader Maria Ohisalo
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maria_Ohisalo> made similar statements,
citing monetary cuts to education, insufficiently counters climate
change and nature loss as major concerns. Swedish People's Party leader
Anna-Maja Henriksson said she did not rule out joining a coalition with
the Finns Party, but stated that she opposed the policies and values
that the Finns pursue. Christian Democratic leader Sari Essayah
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sari_Essayah> said she was open to
joining a coalition led by National Coalition if the coalition agreed to
implement major structural reforms in the labour markets and social
security system.^[101] <#cite_note-coalition7-108> Based on the answers
and initial talks with all parties, Orpo announced that he would
negotiate forming a government with the Finns Party, Swedish People's
Party, and the Christian Democrats.^[102] <#cite_note-109>

Petteri Orpo announced on 15 June that the four parties had agreed to
establish a four-party coalition government <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Orpo_Cabinet>, including the far-right Finns Party.^[103]
<#cite_note-110> The government composition and its nineteen ministerial
posts were announced on 17 June. The National Coalition received eight
posts, the Finns Party seven, and the Swedish People's Party three, with
the remaining one going to the Christian Democrats.^[104] <#cite_note-111>
