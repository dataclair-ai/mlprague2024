Presidential elections were held in the Czech Republic <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_Republic> in January 2023, resulting in the
election of Petr Pavel <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petr_Pavel>.
Incumbent president Miloš Zeman <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Milo%C5%A1_Zeman> was not eligible to run due to the two-term limit
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-term_limit>.

The first round took place on 13 and 14 January. Petr Pavel <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petr_Pavel>, former chair of the NATO Military
Committee <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Chair_of_the_NATO_Military_Committee>, ran as an independent on a pro-
Western <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pro-Western>, pro-European
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pro-European> platform,^[1]
<#cite_note-1> and was one of three candidates backed by the centre-
right governing alliance Spolu <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Spolu_(Czech_Republic)>.^[2] <#cite_note-:1-2> He won the first round of
the election with 35.40% of the popular vote, ahead of Andrej Babiš
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrej_Babi%C5%A1>, the former Czech
prime minister <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_prime_minister>
running as the candidate of ANO 2011 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
ANO_2011>, who finished second with 34.99%.^[3] <#cite_note-:2-3> Babiš
had expressed opposition to Czech support for Ukraine after the Russian
invasion <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine> and was characterised in the media as
using populist <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Populism> rhetoric.^[4]
<#cite_note-4> ^[5] <#cite_note-5> For the runoff, Pavel was backed by
most eliminated candidates and by the incumbent prime minister Petr
Fiala <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petr_Fiala>, while Babiš received
an endorsement from the Communist Party of Bohemia and Moravia <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Bohemia_and_Moravia>^[6]
<#cite_note-6> and the outgoing president Zeman.^[7] <#cite_note-:3-7>

The second round was held on 27 and 28 January. Pavel won the runoff
against Babiš with 58.33% of the vote to become the Czech Republic
president <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_Republic_president>-
elect. He assumed office on 9 March 2023, replacing Zeman.^[8]
<#cite_note-:4-8> ^[9] <#cite_note-inaugurace-9> Babiš conceded defeat
and congratulated Pavel.^[10] <#cite_note-Reuters-10> Voter turnout in
the second round was a little above 70%, the highest in a direct Czech
presidential election and the highest in any national Czech election
since 1998.^[11] <#cite_note-11>

The campaign was widely described in the media as divisive. The outcome
of the election was viewed as a sign of support for the West <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_World> in the context of the War in
Ukraine,^[12] <#cite_note-12> and is expected to strengthen Czech ties
with the European Union <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union>
and the United States <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
United_States>.^[13] <#cite_note-13>


    Background[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=1>]


      Before 2020[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=2>]

Initial speculation about the 2023 election began after the first direct
presidential election in 2013 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2013_Czech_presidential_election>. Political scientist Ladislav Cabada
said that he expected the new president to be younger than Miloš Zeman
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milo%C5%A1_Zeman> when he was elected in
2013. Jiří Dienstbier Jr. <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Ji%C5%99%C3%AD_Dienstbier_Jr.> was often mentioned as a potential left-
wing candidate. Cabada mentioned Petr Fiala <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Petr_Fiala> as a possible right-wing candidate.^[14] <#cite_note-
Příštího_prezidenta_zvolí_Husákovy_děti,_předpovídá_politolog_Cabada-14>
There was also speculation that ANO 2011 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
ANO_2011> leader Andrej Babiš <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Andrej_Babi%C5%A1>, who later became Prime Minister of the Czech
Republic <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Prime_Minister_of_the_Czech_Republic>, might run for president in
2023.^[15] <#cite_note-15> At the previous election <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2018_Czech_presidential_election> in January 2018,
Zeman was elected for his second and final term.^[16] <#cite_note-16>
After Zeman's victory in 2018, there was speculation that the next
election might be held sooner than 2023 due to Zeman's health.^[17]
<#cite_note-
Prezidentské_volby_2018_skončily,_je_čas_začít_myslet_na_ty,_které_budou_možná_už_za_rok_-_Info.cz-17> ^[18] <#cite_note-18> Speculation about possible successors to Zeman started soon after the election, with bookmakers considering Václav Klaus Jr. <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V%C3%A1clav_Klaus_Jr.> as the favourite.^[19] <#cite_note-Sázkové_kanceláře_už_hledají_Zemanova_nástupce._Favoritem_je_Václav_Klaus_ml.-19> Babiš stated that his party would nominate its own candidate for the next election.^[20] <#cite_note-20>

In summer 2019, the leaders of the opposition Civic Democratic Party
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_Democratic_Party_(Czech_Republic)>,
Pirate Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_Pirate_Party>, KDU-ČSL
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KDU-%C4%8CSL>, TOP 09 <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TOP_09> and Mayors and Independents <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mayors_and_Independents> met to discuss a possible
common candidate. The most discussed candidate was retired army general
Petr Pavel <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petr_Pavel>. Other names
included Miroslava Němcová <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Miroslava_N%C4%9Bmcov%C3%A1> and Věra Jourová <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/V%C4%9Bra_Jourov%C3%A1>. The Czech Social Democratic Party
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_Social_Democratic_Party> was also
invited to the meeting but declined as it planned to support Josef
Středula <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josef_St%C5%99edula>, the
president of the Bohemian-Moravian Confederation of Trade Unions
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohemian-
Moravian_Confederation_of_Trade_Unions>.^[21] <#cite_note-
Začalo_hledání_nástupce_Miloše_Zemana._Vstoupit_do_boje_neodmítá_ani_těžká_váha_politických_válek_-_Seznam_Zprávy-21> Speculation about Zeman's health was renewed in October 2019 when Zeman went into hospital and talked about his possible successors, noting Babiš, Josef Středula <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josef_St%C5%99edula>, and Vladimír Dlouhý <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladim%C3%ADr_Dlouh%C3%BD_(politician)>.^[22] <#cite_note-22> ^[23] <#cite_note-23> Senator Marek Hilšer <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marek_Hil%C5%A1er> announced his candidacy on 21 November 2019. He started gathering signatures for his nomination on the same day.^[24] <#cite_note-24>


      After 2020[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=3>]

Pavel launched an initiative called "Stronger Together" in April 2020,
aimed at helping those working in the fight against the coronavirus
pandemic <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
COVID-19_pandemic_in_the_Czech_Republic>, especially in crowdfunding
financial support for volunteers helping in hospitals or creating
medical tools.^[25] <#cite_note-25> Political commentators including
Petr Holec and Ondřej Leinert suggested that the initiative was a launch
of Pavel's presidential bid, noting the similarity with Hillary Clinton
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hillary_Clinton>'s slogan during the 2016
United States presidential election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2016_United_States_presidential_election>.^[26] <#cite_note-26> ^[27]
<#cite_note-27> Pavel said in July 2021 that he planned to run for the
presidency and was preparing his campaign.^[28] <#cite_note-28> Pavel
also visited various regions, which was described by journalists
including Tereza Šídlová as a campaign activity.^[29] <#cite_note-29>

The 2021 legislative election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2021_Czech_legislative_election> resulted in defeat for ANO 2011 and a
majority for the opposition parties in the Chamber of Deputies <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies_of_the_Czech_Republic>. This
prompted speculation that Babiš might run for the presidency following
his defeat in the legislative election.^[30] <#cite_note-30> ^[31]
<#cite_note-31> Following the election on 10 October, Zeman was
hospitalised again, casting doubts over how and when the government
formation talks would be held.^[32] <#cite_note-32> ^[33]
<#cite_note-33> On 11 October, a spokesman for the hospital said that
Zeman's condition had stabilised, but he could not yet talk about the
prognosis: "The reason for [Zeman's] hospitalization are complications
from the illnesses for which he has been receiving treatments."^[34]
<#cite_note-34> Fiala, the leader of the Spolu <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spolu_(Czech_Republic)> coalition, stated on 12
October 2021 that the alliance would nominate a candidate.^[35]
<#cite_note-35>

On 18 October 2021, Miloš Vystrčil <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Milo%C5%A1_Vystr%C4%8Dil>, the president of the Senate of the Czech
Republic <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
President_of_the_Senate_of_the_Czech_Republic>, announced he had
received a letter from the Central Military Hospital declaring Zeman
unfit to fulfil his duties as the president, and indicated plans to
trigger Article 66 of the constitution to temporarily remove Zeman from
office.^[36] <#cite_note-36> In response, the Senate announced their
intention to transfer Zeman's constitutional powers to Babiš and the
speaker of the Chamber of Deputies Radek Vondráček, also of ANO
2011.^[37] <#cite_note-37> However, he was discharged from hospital on
27 November 2021 and resumed his duties.^[38] <#cite_note-38> Babiš
stated on 20 November 2021 that he would run for presidency if his
supporters gather the required 50,000 signatures.^[39] <#cite_note-39>
Security analyst Miloslav Ondra launched an initiative to do so and
started gathering signatures at Anděl metro station on 1 December 2021,
reaching 200 signatures. According to Ondra, he had around 50 gathering
stations across the Czech Republic.^[40] <#cite_note-40>


    Candidates[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=4>]

To qualify for the ballot, a candidate had to gather 50,000 signatures
from citizens or the support of twenty deputies <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Chamber_of_Deputies_of_the_Parliament_of_the_Czech_Republic> or ten
senators <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Senate_of_the_Parliament_of_the_Czech_Republic>. The candidate must file
their application and signatures 66 days before the election, following
which the Interior Ministry <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Ministry_of_the_Interior_(Czech_Republic)> will verify a sample of the
signatures.^[41] <#cite_note-signs-41>

      Withdrawn[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=6>]

  * Josef Středula <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josef_St%C5%99edula>,
    president of the Bohemian-Moravian Confederation of Trade Unions
    <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohemian-
    Moravian_Confederation_of_Trade_Unions> was running with the
    nominations of 11 Senators.^[75] <#cite_note-77> Středula announced
    his candidacy on 5 May 2022,^[76] <#cite_note-78> having long been

    Campaign[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=9>]

On 27 June 2022 the date of election was scheduled for 13 and 14 January
2023,^[109] <#cite_note-111> with the second round scheduled for 27 and
28 January if necessary.^[110] <#cite_note-112>


      Declarations and nominations[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/
      index.php?
      title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=10>]

Janeček, a billionaire entrepreneur and anti-corruption activist,
announced his candidacy on 21 January 2022, launching his campaign with
the slogan "This is Us." Janeček said he was annoyed by the lying,
manipulation and populism in politics.^[111] <#cite_note-113> On 14
February 2022, anti-lockdown activist Jakub Olbert stated that he had
gathered 30,000 signatures for his candidacy.^[112] <#cite_note-114>
Rohanová announced on 18 February that she had submitted her candidacy
signed by 20 MPs of the Chamber of Deputies who were elected in the 2017
election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2017_Czech_legislative_election>, saying she had met the requirements to
qualify for the ballot. Her opinion was challenged by constitutional
lawyer Jan Kysela, who argued that Rohanová could not use the signatures
of former MPs. Rohanová stated that she was still intending to collect
50,000 signatures from citizens, and had already gathered 27,000.^[113]
<#cite_note-115>

Středula announced his candidacy and began collecting signatures on 5
May 2022.^[114] <#cite_note-116> The leader of the Czech Social
Democratic Party, Michal Šmarda <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Michal_%C5%A0marda_(politician)>, announced that his party would likely
support Středula's candidacy.^[115] <#cite_note-
Už_páté_volby_jsou_o_tom,_zda_je_Babiš_hodný_nebo_zlý,_zlobí_šéfa_ČSSD_Šmardu-117> ^[116] <#cite_note-118> He was also endorsed by Zeman.^[117] <#cite_note-119> Former Rector of Charles University <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_University> Zima announced his candidacy on 26 May 2022.^[64] <#cite_note-Zima_oznámil_kandidaturu_na_prezidenta:_Chci_zemi_spokojenou_a_bez_extrémů-66> On 27 May 2022, SPD MEP Blaško announced his intention to run for president.^[118] <#cite_note-120> His candidacy was endorsed by DSSS.^[119] <#cite_note-121> ^[120] <#cite_note-122> Nerudová declared her candidacy on 31 May 2022.^[121] <#cite_note-123> ^[122] <#cite_note-124> On 20 June 2022, Březina announced he had gathered the required 50,000 signatures.^[123] <#cite_note-125>

Pavel confirmed his intention to run on 29 June 2022, saying that he
would launch his campaign officially in August 2022. Pavel stated that
he wanted to win the election so that the Czech Republic would not have
to feel ashamed by its president.^[124] <#cite_note-126> ^[125]
<#cite_note-127> In July 2022 Zima launched his campaign and started
gathering signatures. He used an old ambulance for his campaign to
represent his relationship to the health system.^[126] <#cite_note-128>
Pavel officially announced his candidacy on 6 September 2022 when he
launched his campaign, with the slogan "Let's bring order and peace back
to the Czech Republic".^[127] <#cite_note-129> Karel Janeček launched
his campaign on 7 September 2022 at the VR Play Park. He appeared in
front of journalists in a VR suit to say that anybody could visit his
Metaverse <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaverse> platform to submit
their signature. Janeček was reported to be the first political
candidate to use the Metaverse in his campaign. He said he would appear
in the Metaverse on scheduled occasions to answer questions.^[128]
<#cite_note-130> On 10 September 2022, SPD announced it would nominate
Bašta for the presidency.^[129] <#cite_note-131> ^[130] <#cite_note-132>

On 27 September 2022, Pavel announced he had gathered 55,000 signatures,
enough to participate in the election.^[131] <#cite_note-133> On 3
October 2022, Fischer confirmed his candidacy, saying that he would
launch his campaign in late October 2022.^[132] <#cite_note-134> On 4
October 2022, Spolu leader and Prime Minister Petr Fiala confirmed that
Spolu would not nominate its own candidate, and would instead endorse
three candidates whose views were considered closest to those of ODS,
KDU-ČSL and TOP 09, namely Fischer, Nerudová and Pavel.^[133]
<#cite_note-135> ^[134] <#cite_note-136> ^[135] <#cite_note-137> ^[136]
<#cite_note-138>

On 10 October 2022 Fischer announced that he had gathered 10 signatures
from Senators, allowing him to run.^[137] <#cite_note-139> Karel Janeček
admitted on 11 October 2022 that he had failed to gather 50,000
signatures, so he was seeking nominations from senators.^[138]
<#cite_note-140> On 12 October 2022 it was reported that Cyril Svoboda
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyril_Svoboda>, former leader of KDU-ČSL,
had started gathering signatures in the Senate to allow him to run.
Svoboda initially refused to comment,^[139] <#cite_note-141> but on 13
October confirmed he would not be running as he had failed to gather
enough signatures.^[140] <#cite_note-142> Nerudová announced on the same
day she had gathered enough signatures to submit her candidacy.^[141]
<#cite_note-143> On 14 October 2022 Hilšer confirmed he had gathered the
signatures of 14 Senators allowing him to run.^[58] <#cite_note-
Musíme_vrátit_demokracii_lidem._Hilšer_oficiálně_potvrdil_kandidaturu_na_Hrad_-_Novinky-60> Tomáš Zima also announced on 18 October 2022 that he had collected signatures from 13 senators allowing him to run. Zima said he had collected 37,000 from citizens so far, and could have over 50,000 by November, but had decided to insure himself with senators' signatures.^[142] <#cite_note-144> Pavel Fischer launched his campaign on 18 October 2022, with the slogan "The Czech Republic in First Place".^[143] <#cite_note-145> He said his priorities were respect for tradition, democracy and human rights, society's resistance to hostile influences, economic development, and the economic independence of the Czech Republic.^[144] <#cite_note-146>

On 30 October 2022, Babiš announced his candidacy as the nominee of his
party.^[47] <#cite_note-
Konec_dohadům._Babiš_bude_kandidovat_na_prezidenta_-_Seznam_Zprávy-47>
^[145] <#cite_note-147> Pavel said that Babiš's candidacy was to be
expected. He added that Babiš would breathe new life into the debates
but that his candidacy was "a threat to the Czech Republic". Nerudová
noted that Babiš had been unable to find a better candidate than himself
and decided to run despite forecasts not giving him much chance.^[146]
<#cite_note-148>


      Official campaign[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=11>]

On 8 January 2023, Středula withdrew his candidacy and endorsed
Nerudová.^[147] <#cite_note-149>

Several candidates for president support LGBT rights such as same-sex
marriage and adoptions including: Danuše Nerudová, Petr Pavel, Marek
Hilšer, and Josef Středula. Tomáš Zima also expressed support for same-
sex marriage.^[148] <#cite_note-150>

During the presidential election campaign, Fischer expressed his support
for the right to keep and bear arms <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Right_to_keep_and_bear_arms>, stating that he considered it "correct for
adult citizens to have the basic ability to handle firearms, to know how
to make them safe or check that they are unloaded". Fischer further said
that as president, he would veto restrictions on the legal possession of
firearms by civilians.^[149] <#cite_note-151>

Babiš's prospects in the election were said to have improved at the
beginning of January 2023, after he was cleared by the Municipal Court
in Prague in an alleged fraud case involving misuse of EU
subsidies.^[150] <#cite_note-152>


      Second round[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=12>]

After finishing first in the first round, Pavel was considered to be the
favourite in the second round.^[151] <#cite_note-CNN_Prima_News-153> He
organised rallies in Ústí nad Labem <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
%C3%9Ast%C3%AD_nad_Labem>, Ostrava <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Ostrava>, Brno <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brno> and Prague <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prague>, which were attended by thousands of
people.^[152] <#cite_note-154>

In a press conference after the results were announced, Babiš attacked
Pavel's former past with the Communist Party of Czechoslovakia <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Czechoslovakia>, likening him
to Vladimir Putin <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Putin>, a
statement ridiculed by Babiš's critics.^[153] <#cite_note-:0-155> He
later attempted to portray Pavel as a warmonger;^[154] <#cite_note-
assistance-156> Babiš's billboard campaign saying "I will not drag
Czechia into war. I'm a diplomat, not a soldier." was criticised by the
Czech Minister of Defence Jana Černochová <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Jana_%C4%8Cernochov%C3%A1> and other politicians.^[155]
<#cite_note-157>

Before the second round election, Pavel became the target of several
false claims online, including rumours of his death. Some commentators
described these claims as the largest online disinformation campaign
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disinformation_campaign> in Czech
history.^[156] <#cite_note-158> ^[157] <#cite_note-159> Shortly after
the first round, a string of text messages claiming to be from Pavel,
instructing people to "report to the nearest branch of the Armed Forces"
for conscription in the Russo-Ukrainian war <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Russo-Ukrainian_war>, were reported to the police for
investigation. Pavel denied that he had sent them and accused Babiš's
campaign of organising them.^[158] <#cite_note-160> ^[/citation needed
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed>/] Pavel's
website was attacked by hackers linked to Russia multiple times.^[159]
<#cite_note-161> Meanwhile, Babiš and his family were targeted by
several death threats.^[160] <#cite_note-162>

Babiš's statement during a debate questioning Czech assistance to NATO
allies triggered strong criticism from governments of several NATO
countries, notably Poland <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland> and of
the Baltic states <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_states>,^[154]
<#cite_note-assistance-156> and prompted Pavel to pledge that if elected
he would visit Poland to reassure it that the Czech Republic would
provide military support for its allies if they were attacked.^[161]
<#cite_note-163>

    Endorsements[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=14>]

ANO 2011 and SPD were the only parties to nominate their own candidates.
ANO nominated its leader Babiš, while SPD nominated Bašta, a member of
the Chamber of Deputies.^[175] <#cite_note-177> ^[176] <#cite_note-178>
^[177] <#cite_note-179> ^[178] <#cite_note-180>

ODS, KDU-ČSL and TOP 09 confirmed in September 2022 that they would not
nominate a joint candidate, and decided to endorse three candidates:
Fischer, Nerudová and Pavel.^[179] <#cite_note-PetrPavelSpolu-181> ČSSD
endorsed Josef Středula,^[115] <#cite_note-
Už_páté_volby_jsou_o_tom,_zda_je_Babiš_hodný_nebo_zlý,_zlobí_šéfa_ČSSD_Šmardu-117> who subsequently withdrew and endorsed Nerudová.

The Czech Pirate Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Czech_Pirate_Party> launched a survey of party members to determine
which candidate to endorse.^[180] <#cite_note-182> Pavel led the ballot
with 37% of votes. Nerudová finished second with 30% while Hilšer was
third with 15%. The party decided not to endorse any candidate, but
advised its supporters to vote for a candidate who would bring dignity
to the institution of the presidency, and unite people and honour the
constitution and law instead of dividing society.^[181] <#cite_note-183>

The Communist Party of Bohemia and Moravia <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Communist_Party_of_Bohemia_and_Moravia> nominated Skála, who failed
to gather enough signatures and withdrew.^[182] <#cite_note-184> Party
leader Kateřina Konečná <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Kate%C5%99ina_Kone%C4%8Dn%C3%A1> subsequently stated that the party
would only endorse a candidate who asked for support.^[183]
<#cite_note-185>

The Tricolour Citizens' Movement <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Tricolour_Citizens%27_Movement>, Alliance of National Forces <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alliance_of_National_Forces_(Czech_Republic)>
(ANS), PES Movement <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
title=PES_Movement&action=edit&redlink=1> and Manifest.cz <https://
en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Manifest.cz&action=edit&redlink=1>
endorsed Bašta.^[184] <#cite_note-186> Moravané <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moravan%C3%A9> sent a questionnaire to all
candidates to determine which candidate to endorse.^[185]
<#cite_note-187> The Workers' Party of Social Justice <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Workers%27_Party_of_Social_Justice> endorsed
Blaško, who failed to gather enough signatures,^[186] <#cite_note-188>
and eventually endorsed Bašta.^[187] <#cite_note-189>

Svobodní <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Svobodn%C3%AD> held a survey
among its members. Janeček, who had already been disqualified from the
election, was the most popular candidate with 28.26% of votes. 27.83%
voted to support nobody, 18.70% voted to support Bašta, 6.09% voted to
support Diviš, 5.22% voted to support Pavel, 4.78% voted to support
Nerudová, and 4.35% voted to support Fischer. The party decided not to
endorse any candidate, stating that none of them shared the party's
values.^[188] <#cite_note-190>

    Results[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=22>]

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:
2023_Presidential_Elections_in_Czechia.jpg>
Voting in 2023 Czech presidential election, electoral district no. 70,
Olomouc <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olomouc>

The first round of the Czech presidential election took place on 13 and
14 January. Petr Pavel, former chair of the NATO Military Committee
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chair_of_the_NATO_Military_Committee>,
ran as an independent with the endorsement of the governing alliance
SPOLU.^[2] <#cite_note-:1-2> He won the first round with 35.4 per cent
of the vote, while former prime minister Babiš running as the candidate
of ANO 2011 finished second with 35 per cent.^[3] <#cite_note-:2-3>
Pavel was subsequently endorsed by four of the eliminated candidates,
while Bašta and Zima did not endorse either candidates. Pavel was also
endorsed by the incumbent prime minister Petr Fiala, while Babiš
received endorsements from the Communist Party <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Bohemia_and_Moravia>, the
Tricolour Movement <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Tricolour_Citizens%27_Movement>, and outgoing president Zeman.^[7]
<#cite_note-:3-7>

The runoff was held on 27 and 28 January. Pavel defeated Babiš with 58
per cent of the vote, and became the president-elect. He will assume
office on 9 March, replacing Zeman.^[9] <#cite_note-inaugurace-9> ^[8]
<#cite_note-:4-8>

On 29 January, an electoral district in Prague-Bohnice <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohnice> was reported to have accidentally
submitted its reported tallies for Babiš and Pavel the wrong way round,
resulting in about 100 votes being erroneously counted in favour of
Babiš. However, the legal period during which electoral commissions can
still correct the submitted results had already expired when the error
was noticed, after which only the Supreme Administrative Court can order
the correction of the official results.^[194] <#cite_note-196> ^[195]
<#cite_note-197>

    Aftermath[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Czech_presidential_election&action=edit&section=26>]

Babiš conceded defeat in a speech at his headquarters, and congratulated
Pavel, expressing hope that Pavel would be "everyone's president".^[196]
<#cite_note-198> Former Minister of Finance Alena Schillerová <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alena_Schillerov%C3%A1> called Babiš's result a
"historic result for ANO" which the party would try to replicate in the
next parliamentary election.^[197] <#cite_note-after-199> Prime Minister
Petr Fiala <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petr_Fiala> also sent a
congratulatory message,^[10] <#cite_note-Reuters-10> and criticised
Babiš's campaign during the election, calling it "the most abhorrent in
our new era" and hailed "the end of Babiš's era in our country".
Outgoing president Miloš Zeman <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Milo%C5%A1_Zeman> expressed surprise at the scale of Pavel's victory,
and stated that Babiš "should not be written off prematurely".^[197]
<#cite_note-after-199> Pavel was also congratulated by Senate leader
Miloš Vystrčil <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milo%C5%A1_Vystr%C4%8Dil>,
Chamber of Deputies speaker Markéta Pekarová Adamová <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark%C3%A9ta_Pekarov%C3%A1_Adamov%C3%A1>, and
Deputy Prime Minister Marian Jurečka <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Marian_Jure%C4%8Dka>. Ivan Bartoš <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Ivan_Barto%C5%A1>, the leader of the Czech Pirate Party <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_Pirate_Party>, called upon Babiš to
apologise for what he described as his "dishonest" campaign.^[197]
<#cite_note-after-199> On 3 February 2023 Karel Janeček <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karel_Jane%C4%8Dek> submitted a complaint to the
Supreme Administrative Court over the presidential election
process.^[198] <#cite_note-200>
