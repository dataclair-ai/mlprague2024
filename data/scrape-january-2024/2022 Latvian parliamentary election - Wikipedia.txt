Parliamentary elections were held in Latvia <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Latvia> on 1 October 2022, following the end of the term of the
13th Saeima <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saeima> elected in 2018.


    Electoral system[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2022_Latvian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=1>]

The 100 members of the Saeima are elected by open list <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_list>, proportional representation <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportional_representation> from five multi-
member constituencies ranging in size from 12 to 36 seats and based on
the regions of Latvia <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regions_of_Latvia>,
with overseas votes included in the Riga constituency. Seats are
allocated using the Sainte-Laguë method <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Sainte-Lagu%C3%AB_method> with a national electoral threshold <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_threshold> of 5%. Voters may cast
"specific votes" for candidates on the list that they have voted for.
This involves drawing a plus sign (+) next to the candidate's names to
indicate preference (positive votes), or by crossing out names to
indicate disapproval (negative votes). The number of votes for each
candidate is the number of votes cast for the list, plus their number of
positive votes, minus their number of negative votes. The candidates
with the highest vote totals fill their party's seats.^[1] <#cite_note-1>


      Seat redistribution[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2022_Latvian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=2>]

The Central Electoral Commission is required to determine the number of
Members of Parliament (MPs) to be elected using the number of eligible
voters four months before the election. On 2 June 2022, the Central
Electoral Commission has announced the new distribution of MPs. Rīga
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riga_(Saeima_constituency)> and Vidzeme
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livonia_(Saeima_constituency)>
constituencies have both gained one seat compared to the 2018 election,
while Latgale <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Latgallia_(Saeima_constituency)> and Zemgale <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Semigallia_(Saeima_constituency)> constituencies have both lost
one.^[2] <#cite_note-2>

      Lead candidates[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2022_Latvian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=4>]

Latvia First became the first major party to announce Ainārs Šlesers
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ain%C4%81rs_%C5%A0lesers> as its
candidate for the position of Prime Minister <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Latvia> on 14 August, during the founding
congress of the party.^[3] <#cite_note-3>

Each of the member parties of the Union of Greens and Farmers proposed
their own PM candidate to the party alliance board, and then they would
decide on the one candidate for the whole party alliance. The Latvian
Farmers' Union <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latvian_Farmers%27_Union>
nominated MP Viktors Valainis <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
title=Viktors_Valainis&action=edit&redlink=1> as their candidate,^[4]
<#cite_note-4> while the Green Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Latvian_Green_Party> kept its candidate secret.^[5] <#cite_note-5> In
the end, the Greens left the Union and joined the United List <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_List_(Latvia)> alliance.^[6] <#cite_note-6>

    Results[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2022_Latvian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=9>]

The New Unity <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Unity> party of
incumbent prime minister Krišjānis Kariņš <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Kri%C5%A1j%C4%81nis_Kari%C5%86%C5%A1> received the highest
percentage of the vote (19%) and won the most seats (26). In a speech
after the election, Kariņš stated that Latvia would continue to support
Ukraine against Russia and he stated his preference to maintain the
current coalition government <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
First_Kari%C5%86%C5%A1_cabinet>.^[38] <#cite_note-38> ^[39]
<#cite_note-:7-39> The Union of Greens and Farmers <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_of_Greens_and_Farmers> placed second,
receiving 13% of the vote, despite leader Aivars Lembergs <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aivars_Lembergs> being sentenced to five years in
prison in 2021 and under sanction by the United States.^[40]
<#cite_note-:6-40> The other parties which placed above the 5% threshold
to receive a seat in parliament were the United List <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_List_(Latvia)> with 11%, the National
Alliance <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Alliance_(Latvia)> with
9.3%, For Stability! <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/For_Stability!> with
7%, Latvia First <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latvia_First> with 6%,
and The Progressives <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
The_Progressives_(Latvia)>, who entered parliament for the first time
with 6% of the vote.^[40] <#cite_note-:6-40> ^[39] <#cite_note-:7-39>

The wasted vote <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wasted_vote> in this
election was 29.09%. Harmony <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Social_Democratic_Party_%22Harmony%22>, who had placed first in the
previous three general elections, was unable to secure any parliamentary
seats, being slightly under the 5% threshold (of all votes, including
invalid) with 4.9% of the vote. This has been attributed by many
reasons, including internal disputes on the 2022 Russian invasion of
Ukraine <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine>, pushing away both ethnic Latvians and
ethnic Russians <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians_in_Latvia>. This
resulted in much of the ethnic Russian population voting for Stability!,
Sovereign Power <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Sovereign_Power_(Latvia)>, and the Latvian Russian Union <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latvian_Russian_Union>.^[41] <#cite_note-41> ^[40]
<#cite_note-:6-40> ^[42] <#cite_note-42> The Development/For! <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Development/For!> alliance, one of the coalition
members, also narrowly missed out the 5% threshold by just 0.03% (with
4.97% of all votes).^[39] <#cite_note-:7-39> Another coalition member,
the Conservatives, also failed to cross the 5% threshold, receiving 3%
of the vote.^[40] <#cite_note-:6-40>

    Aftermath[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2022_Latvian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=12>]

On 3 October 2022, President Egils Levits <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Egils_Levits> authorized Kariņš, the incumbent Prime Minister and
leader of New Unity, to form a coalition government.^[44]
<#cite_note-44> New Unity explored coalition options with Union of
Greens and Farmers and the Progressives, but they fell though.^[45]
<#cite_note-45> ^[46] <#cite_note-46> Kariņš formed a government
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Kari%C5%86%C5%A1_cabinet> with
United List and National Alliance, sworn in on 14 December.^[47]
<#cite_note-47> ^[48] <#cite_note-48> This government lasted until
proposed changes to the make-up of cabinet caused the other parties to
pull out and led to Kariņš's resignation as Prime Minister in August
2023. The government was succeeded by a New Unity–Union of Greens and
Farmers–Progressives government led by Evika Siliņa <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sili%C5%86a_cabinet>, which was sworn in on 14
September 2023.^[49] <#cite_note-49>
