Presidential elections were held in France <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/France> on 10 and 24 April 2022. As no candidate won a majority
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majority> in the first round, a runoff
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-round_system> was held, in which
Emmanuel Macron <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron> defeated
Marine Le Pen <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_Le_Pen> and was re-
elected as President of France <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
President_of_France>.^[1] <#cite_note-:3-1> Macron, from La République
En Marche! <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_R%C3%A9publique_En_Marche!>
(LREM), had defeated Le Pen, leader of the National Rally <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Rally>, once already in the 2017 French
presidential election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2017_French_presidential_election>, for the term which expired on 13 May
2022. Macron became the first president of France to win a re-election
bid since Jacques Chirac <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Chirac>
won in 2002 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2002_French_presidential_election>.^[2] <#cite_note-2>

In the first round, Macron took the lead with 27.9% of votes, followed
by Le Pen with 23.2%, Jean-Luc Mélenchon <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Jean-Luc_M%C3%A9lenchon> of La France Insoumise <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_France_Insoumise> with 22%, and Éric Zemmour
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89ric_Zemmour> of Reconquête
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reconqu%C3%AAte> with 7.1%. Valérie
Pécresse <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Val%C3%A9rie_P%C3%A9cresse> of
The Republicans <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Republicans_(France)>
took 4.8% of the vote, and Anne Hidalgo <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Anne_Hidalgo>, mayor of Paris <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Mayor_of_Paris> and Socialist Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Socialist_Party_(France)> candidate, 1.8%. Both the Republicans and
Socialist parties, considered to be the dominant parties until 2017,^[3]
<#cite_note-3> received their worst results in a presidential
election.^[4] <#cite_note-4>

In the second round, Macron beat Le Pen with 58.5% of the vote to her
41.5%, a narrower margin than in the 2017 election. Turnout was 72.0%,
the lowest in a presidential election run-off since 1969 <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1969_French_presidential_election>.^[5]
<#cite_note-5> Le Pen conceded defeat after exit projections <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exit_projections> became available. The
presidential election was followed by the 2022 French legislative
election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2022_French_legislative_election>, held on 12–19 June, to elect the 577
members <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_of_Parliament_(France)> of
the National Assembly <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
National_Assembly_(France)>, the lower house of the French Parliament
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Parliament>.


    Electoral system[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2022_French_presidential_election&action=edit&section=1>]

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Logo_PR2022.svg>
Official logo of the election

Under Article 7 of the Constitution of France <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Constitution_of_France>, the president <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/President_of_France> is elected to a five-year term <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidential_elections_in_France> in a two-round
election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two_round_voting_system>.^[6]
<#cite_note-6> If no candidate secures an absolute majority of votes in
the first round, a second round is held two weeks later between the two
candidates who received the most votes.^[7] <#cite_note-:2-7> According
to the Constitution of France, the first round of the presidential
election must be held between 20 and 35 days before the transition of
power at the end of the five-year term of the incumbent officeholder.^[/
citation needed <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Wikipedia:Citation_needed>/] As Emmanuel Macron <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron> took office on 14 May 2017, the
transition of power is expected to take place on 13 May 2022.
Correspondingly, the first round of the presidential election was to be
held between 8 and 23 April 2022, with the second round held two weeks
after the first.^[8] <#cite_note-8> On 13 July 2021, Government
Spokesman Gabriel Attal <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabriel_Attal>
announced the dates for the election, respectively 10 April 2022 for the
first round and 24 April 2022 for the eventual second round.^[9]
<#cite_note-9>

To be listed on the first-round ballot, candidates needed to secure 500
signatures (often referred to as /parrainages/ in French) from national
or local elected officials from at least 30 different departments
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Departments_of_France> or overseas
collectivities <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_collectivity>,
with no more than a tenth of these signatories from any single
department. The signatures were submitted to the Constitutional Council
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_Council_(France)>, which
is the sole authority to designate participants.^[10] <#cite_note-10>

      Campaign[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2022_French_presidential_election&action=edit&section=3>]

Following the 2017 presidential election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2017_French_presidential_election>, The Republicans <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Republicans_(France)> (LR) sent its members a
questionnaire on the topic of the "re-foundation" of the party; of the
40,000 respondents, 70% voted against an open primary like that which
was held in 2016 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2016_The_Republicans_(France)_presidential_primary> to determine the
party nominee.^[11] <#cite_note-11> In a document dated 17 October 2017,
the Socialist Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Socialist_Party_(France)> (PS) wrote that the financing of the 2022
presidential campaign was not assured despite "economic restructuring"
but still planned to spend €12,000,000, the maximum legally permitted
before the first round. According to the report, the party's leadership
had seriously considered the possibility of not presenting a PS
candidate in 2022.^[12] <#cite_note-12>

Marine Le Pen <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_Le_Pen>, the
president of the National Rally <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
National_Rally> (RN), announced on 16 January 2020 that she would be
running in the election. She previously ran in the 2012 <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_French_presidential_election> and 2017
presidential elections as the party's candidate, then called the
National Front (FN). She came third in 2012 with 17.9% of the vote in
the first round and second in 2017 with 21.3% of the vote in the first
round and 33.9% of the vote in the second round. Le Pen was elected to
the National Assembly <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
National_Assembly_(France)> in the 2017 French legislative election
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2017_French_legislative_election>.^[13]
<#cite_note-13>

Jean Lassalle <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Lassalle>, who ran in
the 2017 presidential election under the Résistons! banner, coming in
seventh place with 1.2% of the vote, announced that he would run
again.^[14] <#cite_note-14> In 2020, MP Joachim Son-Forget <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joachim_Son-Forget>, a radiologist who was elected
to the National Assembly for La République En Marche! <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_R%C3%A9publique_En_Marche!> (LREM) in 2017,
formed a new political party called Valeur Absolue and announced his
intention to enter the race for the presidency. He had resigned from the
LREM group <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
La_R%C3%A9publique_En_Marche_group_(National_Assembly)> after posting
tweets in 2018 that were deemed sexist; he then joined the UDI and
Independents group <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
UDI_and_Independents_group> in 2019 before resigning his membership
later that year.^[15] <#cite_note-15>

On 8 November 2020, Jean-Luc Mélenchon <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Jean-Luc_M%C3%A9lenchon>, founder of La France Insoumise <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_France_Insoumise> (LFI), announced that he
would be running in the election. He previously ran in the 2012
presidential election for the Left Front <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Left_Front_(France)> (coming fourth with 11.1% of the vote in the first
round) and in the 2017 presidential election for LFI (coming fourth
again with 19.5% of the vote in the first round). Mélenchon was elected
to the National Assembly in 2017.^[16] <#cite_note-16>

In November 2021, Ensemble Citoyens <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Ensemble_Citoyens> was founded. It is a political coalition <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_coalition> composed of the presidential
majority led under Emmanuel Macron <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Emmanuel_Macron>.^[17] <#cite_note-17>

In January 2022, Éric Zemmour <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
%C3%89ric_Zemmour>'s party Reconquête <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Reconqu%C3%AAte>, which was founded the month prior, gained a member of
the National Assembly in Guillaume Peltier <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Guillaume_Peltier>, previously elected as a member of LR,^[18]
<#cite_note-18> as well as two Members of the European Parliament
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament> (MEPs)
when Jérôme Rivière <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Rivi%C3%A8re> and Gilbert Collard <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilbert_Collard> defected from Le Pen's RN.^[19]
<#cite_note-19> ^[20] <#cite_note-20> Previously, Son-Forget, who had
declared he would run for the presidency, rallied behind Zemmour's
candidacy. In early February 2022, the party gained a third MEP when
Maxette Grisoni-Pirbakas <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxette_Grisoni-
Pirbakas> defected from the RN.^[21] <#cite_note-21> Stéphane Ravier
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St%C3%A9phane_Ravier> became Zemmour's
first supporter in the Senate <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Senate_(France)> after he left the RN mid-February 2022.^[22]
<#cite_note-22>

In February 2022, a wave of defections hit Valérie Pécresse <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Val%C3%A9rie_P%C3%A9cresse>, candidate put forward
by LR, in favour of Macron.^[23] <#cite_note-23> She was accused by
members of the party's centrist wing of trying to pander to the voters
of Zemmour,^[24] <#cite_note-24> whose sharp rise in the polls has been
qualified as "meteoric".^[25] <#cite_note-25> During a rally in February
2022, Pécresse said "in ten years time ... will we be a sovereign
nation, a US satellite or a Chinese trading post? Will we be unified or
divided? Nothing is written, whether it is loss of economic status, or
the Great Replacement <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Great_Replacement>." She was criticised for referring to the Great
Replacement; she later said that her mention was not an endorsement of
what she considered to be a "theory of hate".^[26] <#cite_note-26> ^[27]
<#cite_note-27> ^[28] <#cite_note-28> ^[29] <#cite_note-29>

The 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine> that began on 24 February had
significant implications for the campaign. As media coverage switched to
covering the war, Macron's polling improved significantly during the
crisis.^[30] <#cite_note-30> Le Pen and Zemmour were made to explain
historic statements of praise for Vladimir Putin <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Putin>.^[31] <#cite_note-31> ^[32]
<#cite_note-32> ^[33] <#cite_note-33> In a 14 March 2022 interview with
newspaper /Le Figaro <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Figaro>/, Gérard
Larcher <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Larcher>, Senate
President <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
List_of_presidents_of_the_Senate_of_France> and a supporter of Pécresse,
put into question the legitimacy of a possible second Macron term,
stating: "If there is no campaign, the question of the legitimacy of the
winner will arise."^[34] <#cite_note-34> Those comments echoed Macron's
refusal to participate in any debate with the other candidates prior to
the election's first round.^[35] <#cite_note-35>

Macron formally announced his candidacy for re-election on 3 March 2022,
by which time he had already received well more than the sponsorships
from elected officials <#required_number_of_sponsorships> to qualify for
the ballot.

Marion Maréchal <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marion_Mar%C3%A9chal> of
the Le Pen family <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Pen_family>,
granddaughter of FN founder Jean-Marie Le Pen <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Jean-Marie_Le_Pen> and niece of its current leader Marine Le Pen,
formalised her support for Zemmour at a large rally in Toulon <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toulon> on 6 March 2022.^[36] <#cite_note-36>
^[37] <#cite_note-37> In the final days before the first round of
voting, Le Pen's polling numbers improved to within the margin of error
of defeating Macron in the second round, while those of Pécresse and
Zemmour fell.^[38] <#cite_note-38> ^[39] <#cite_note-39> ^[40]
<#cite_note-40>

Mélenchon's polling numbers also surged in the final days of
campaigning.^[41] <#cite_note-41> ^[42] <#cite_note-42> ^[43]
<#cite_note-43> Left-leaning independent candidate Christiane Taubira
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiane_Taubira>, former Minister of
Justice <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_(France)>
(2012–2014) under President François Hollande <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Hollande> and winner of the 2022 People's Primary
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_French_People%27s_Primary> vote,
withdrew her candidacy on 2 March 2022, endorsing Mélenchon.^[44]
<#cite_note-44>

        People's Primary[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
        title=2022_French_presidential_election&action=edit&section=7>]

Independent activists launched a primary with the intention of
nominating a unity left-wing candidate. The voting took place online
from 27 to 30 January 2022. Of the seven candidates listed in the
primary, three declined to participate.^[51] <#cite_note-51> The primary
was conducted according to a majority judgment <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majority_judgment> voting system, in which all
voters are to rate all candidates, with the candidate with the highest
median rating winning.^[52] <#cite_note-52>

A candidate must have secured 500 /Présentation/ signatures from elected
officials in order to appear on the first-round ballot. The signature
collection period ended on 4 March.^[56] <#cite_note-56> The table below
lists sponsorships received by the Constitutional Council by
candidate.^[57] <#cite_note-parrainages-par-candidat-57> On the form,
this is named a /présentation/ but is more widely known as /
parrainage/.^[58] <#cite_note-58> There were 46 individuals who received
at least 1 sponsorship as of the closing date deadline of 4 March 2022.
Some of them received sponsorships without being candidates, and one
sponsored himself. Candidates labeled SE (/sans etiquette/) do not
belong to any political party.

      Campaign[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2022_French_presidential_election&action=edit&section=14>]

On 14 April 2022, Le Pen said if elected she would hold a referendum
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Referendum> on whether to reinstate
capital punishment in France <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Capital_punishment_in_France>, if such a proposal garnered enough
signatures under the citizens' initiative referendum <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizens%27_initiative_referendum_(France)> system
she wants to implement.^[71] <#cite_note-72> ^[72] <#cite_note-73> Le
Pen had also campaigned for a ban on wearing Muslim headscarves
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hijab> in public.^[73] <#cite_note-74>
^[74] <#cite_note-75>

On 20 April, the only election debate of the campaign (moderated by Léa
Salamé <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9a_Salam%C3%A9> and Gilles
Bouleau <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilles_Bouleau>) to feature both
major candidates was held. Polls conducted after the debate to ascertain
which candidate performed best, showed that 59% of viewers thought that
Macron had performed better, compared to 39% for Le Pen.^[75]
<#cite_note-76> ^[76] <#cite_note-77>

    Results[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2022_French_presidential_election&action=edit&section=19>]

Macron was re-elected with 58.55% of the vote to 41.45% for Le Pen in
the second round of the election.^[78] <#cite_note-results-79> Exit poll
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exit_poll> projections by Ipsos <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ipsos> and Sopra Steria <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Sopra_Steria> for France Télévisions <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/France_T%C3%A9l%C3%A9visions> and Radio France <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_France>, released as voting closed,
estimated that Macron defeated Le Pen with 58.2% of the vote to
41.8%.^[79] <#cite_note-80> He became the first French president to win
re-election since Jacques Chirac <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Jacques_Chirac> in 2002 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2002_French_presidential_election>.^[80] <#cite_note-81> ^[81]
<#cite_note-NYT0424-82> ^[82] <#cite_note-83> The projections, based on
actual ballot papers, also showed that 28% of registered voters did not
show up to the second round,^[83] <#cite_note-wapo0424-84> making it the
lowest turnout since 1969 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
1969_French_presidential_election>.^[84] <#cite_note-smh0424-85>
Official results showed that the turnout was 71.99%, with over 13
million abstentions in the second round, in addition to over 8.6% of
ballots cast being blank or invalid (a marked increase over the first
round).^[78] <#cite_note-results-79>

    Aftermath[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2022_French_presidential_election&action=edit&section=26>]

/The New York Times <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_York_Times>/
commented that the race was much closer than in 2017, when Macron won
66.1% of the vote to Le Pen's 33.9%, but that Macron's margin was wider
than expected prior to the election.^[86] <#cite_note-NYT04242-87> Le
Pen conceded defeat minutes after the estimated results were
released,^[84] <#cite_note-smh0424-85> but still called the outcome a
victory for her political movement and for the upcoming parliamentary
elections.^[87] <#cite_note-skyblog2-88> ^[1] <#cite_note-:3-1> It was
the best result for the far right in France <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Far_right_in_France> since the founding of the Fifth French
Republic <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fifth_French_Republic> in 1958
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1958_French_presidential_election>.^[88]
<#cite_note-89>

Olivier Véran <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olivier_V%C3%A9ran>,
Minister for Solidarity and Health <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Minister_of_Health_(France)>, stated that the government has "heard the
French people's message", referring to the increasing number of votes
for the far-right, and that "there will be a change of method".^[87]
<#cite_note-skyblog2-88> Macron reflected on the results of the
elections self-critically, assuming that many voters voted for him to
counter the far right rather than in support of his political
positions.^[89] <#cite_note-:4-90> Macron was congratulated by several
world leaders on his re-election, with his first call coming from German
chancellor Olaf Scholz <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olaf_Scholz>.^[90]
