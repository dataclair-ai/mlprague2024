Parliamentary elections were held in Hungary <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Hungary> on 3 April 2022 to elect the National Assembly <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_(Hungary)>, coinciding with a
referendum <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2022_Hungarian_LGBT_in_education_referendum>.^[1] <#cite_note-2> ^[2]
<#cite_note-3>

Hungary's incumbent prime minister Viktor Orbán <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viktor_Orb%C3%A1n> won re-election to a fourth
term. Addressing his supporters after the partial results showed his
Fidesz party leading by a wide margin, Orbán said: "We won a victory so
big that you can see it from the moon, and you can certainly see it from
Brussels".^[3] <#cite_note-:0-4> Opposition leader Péter Márki-Zay
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P%C3%A9ter_M%C3%A1rki-Zay> admitted
defeat shortly after Orbán's speech.^[4] <#cite_note-:1-5> /Reuters
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuters>/ described it as a "crushing
victory".^[5] <#cite_note-:2-6>

The election had been predicted to be closer than in previous years, but
Fidesz still held a 5–6 percentage point lead in the polls leading up to
the vote.^[6] <#cite_note-:4-7> OSCE <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Organization_for_Security_and_Co-operation_in_Europe> deployed a full
monitoring mission for the vote.^[7] <#cite_note-:5-8> With Orbán
seeking a fourth consecutive term in office, preliminary results showed
his party Fidesz <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fidesz> outperforming
polls, winning its first absolute majority of the vote share since 2010
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Hungarian_parliamentary_election>
while expanding its supermajority to control 135 seats of the 199-seat
Parliament, comfortably ahead of the opposition alliance United for
Hungary <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_for_Hungary>, which was
set to win 57 seats after 100% of the votes had been counted.^[8]
<#cite_note-:3-9> The Mi Hazánk <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Our_Homeland_Movement> party won seats for the first time, obtaining 6
seats.^[9] <#cite_note-hungarytoday.hu-10>

With 54.13% of the popular vote, Fidesz received the highest vote share
by any party since the fall of communism in 1989.


    Background[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2022_Hungarian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=1>]


      Amendment of the electoral law[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/
      index.php?
      title=2022_Hungarian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=2>]

The electoral law, under which the previous two elections were held
(2014 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2014_Hungarian_parliamentary_election>, 2018 <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/2018_Hungarian_parliamentary_election>), specified the number of
constituency candidates a party needed to run nationwide at 27, in at
least 9 counties.

On 15 December 2020, the National Assembly, in which the governing
parties held the needed 2/3 majority, voted to increase the requirements
to a total of 71 constituency candidates in at least 14 counties and the
capital <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budapest>.^[10] <#cite_note-11>
^[11] <#cite_note-12>


      Creation of joint opposition list[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/
      w/index.php?
      title=2022_Hungarian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=3>]

In 2019 local elections <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2019_Hungarian_local_elections>, Democratic Coalition <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Coalition_(Hungary)>, Jobbik <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jobbik>, LMP <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
LMP_%E2%80%93_Hungary%27s_Green_Party>, MSZP <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Hungarian_Socialist_Party>, Momentum Movement <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum_Movement>, Dialogue <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialogue_for_Hungary> and numerous other parties
or independent candidates ran in joint lists. It enabled opposition
parties to win the majority in the General Assembly of Budapest
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Assembly_of_Budapest> and in ten
of the 23 cities with county rights.^[12] <#cite_note-13>

On 13 August 2020, Democratic Coalition, Jobbik, LMP, MSZP, Momentum
Movement, MLP <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Hungarian_Liberal_Party>,^[13] <#cite_note-14> UK <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Start_(Hungary)>^[14] <#cite_note-15> and
Dialogue decided to field joint candidates in all of Hungary's 106
constituencies and launch a joint program for the elections.^[15]
<#cite_note-16>

On 15 November 2020, Democratic Coalition, Jobbik, LMP, MSZP, Momentum
Movement and Dialogue agreed on running with a common candidate for
Prime Minister <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Prime_Minister_of_Hungary>, to be selected in a primary election
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021_Hungarian_opposition_primary>. They
expect the candidate to be selected by 23 October 2021.^[16]
<#cite_note-17>

On 20 December 2020, Democratic Coalition, Jobbik, LMP, MSZP, Momentum
Movement and Dialogue decided on running together on a common list,
solidifying their electoral alliance.^[17] <#cite_note-18> ^[18]
<#cite_note-19> The leaders of the respective parties have also signed a
declaration containing the terms of their cooperation, titled
"Korszakváltás Garanciái" ("Principles for Hungary's governance after
2022"). The opposition coalition was very ideologically diverse,
something described by observers as a potential hindrance to presenting
a united front to defeat Orbán.^[19] <#cite_note-20>


      Opposition primary[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2022_Hungarian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=4>]

The Opposition <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_for_Hungary>
primary was held in Hungary <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungary>,
between 12 and 27 September 2021 (first round) and 10–16 October 2021
(second round), to select the candidate for Prime Minister of Hungary
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Hungary> supported by
the opposition parties to form a coalition to compete in the 2022
Hungarian parliamentary election. It was the first countrywide primary
election in the political history of Hungary.

On 17 October 2021, non-partisan candidate Péter Márki-Zay <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P%C3%A9ter_M%C3%A1rki-Zay> won in the runoff
(second round) of the opposition primary <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2021_Hungarian_opposition_primary>, becoming the United Opposition
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_for_Hungary> candidate in the
election for prime minister. The parties had also selected common
candidates for single-member districts <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Single-member_district> via the primary in the first round.^[20]
<#cite_note-21>


    Electoral system[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2022_Hungarian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=5>]

The 199 members of the National Assembly <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
National_Assembly_(Hungary)> are to be elected by mixed-member
majoritarian representation <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed-
member_majoritarian_representation> with two methods; 106 are elected in
single-member constituencies by first-past-the-post voting <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting>, with the remaining 93
elected from a single nationwide constituency mostly by proportional
representation <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Proportional_representation>, via a partially compensatory system
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scorporo> (a hybrid of parallel voting
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_voting> and mixed single vote
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_single_vote>). The electoral
threshold is set at 5%, although this is raised to 10% for coalitions of
two parties and 15% for coalitions of three or more parties. Seats are
allocated using the d'Hondt method <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
D%27Hondt_method>.^[21] <#cite_note-22>

The governing party is allowed to draw the boundaries of the single-
member constituencies, and the boundaries were gerrymandered by the
Fidesz government prior to the 2014 elections. The redrawing was done in
a way that smaller constituencies (with fewer than 70,000 voters) had
higher concentrations of Fidesz voters than larger constituencies (over
80,000 voters), meaning fewer votes were required to elect Fidesz
constituency candidates.^[22] <#cite_note-23> In 2010 seats varied in
population by 35%.^[23] <#cite_note-24>

Since 2014 each of the Armenian, Bulgarian, Croatian, German, Greek,
Polish, Romani, Romanian, Rusyn <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Rusyn_people>, Serbian, Slovak, Slovenian, and Ukrainian ethnic
minorities can win one of the 93 party lists seats if they register as a
specific list and reach a lowered quota of {\displaystyle {\frac {1}
{4\times 93}}={\frac {1}{372}}\approx 0.27\%} of the total of party list
votes. Each minority is able to send a minority spokesman – without the
rights of a MP – to the National Assembly, if the list does not reach
this lowered quota.^[24] <#cite_note-25>

Due to internal disputes, the National Self-Government of Romani did not
participate in the election.^[25] <#cite_note-26>

    Aftermath[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2022_Hungarian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=11>]

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Peace_and_Security_(1).png>
Viktor Orbán celebrating his victory

Hungary's incumbent prime minister Viktor Orbán <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viktor_Orb%C3%A1n> declared victory on Sunday
night, with partial results showing his Fidesz party leading the vote by
a wide margin. Addressing his supporters after the partial results,
Orbán said: "We won a victory so big that you can see it from the moon,
and you can certainly see it from Brussels".^[3] <#cite_note-:0-4> Orbán
would go on to declare his victory as a rebuke against the Hungarian
left, international media, George Soros <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
George_Soros>, the "Brussels bureaucrats", and Volodymyr Zelenskyy
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volodymyr_Zelenskyy>.^[28]
<#cite_note-32> ^[29] <#cite_note-33> ^[30] <#cite_note-34> Opposition
leader Péter Márki-Zay <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
P%C3%A9ter_M%C3%A1rki-Zay> admitted defeat shortly after Orbán's
speech.^[4] <#cite_note-:1-5> /Reuters <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Reuters>/ described it as a "crushing victory."^[5] <#cite_note-:2-6>

The election had been predicted to be closer than in previous years, but
Fidesz still held a 5–6 percentage point lead in the polls leading up to
the vote.^[6] <#cite_note-:4-7> OSCE <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Organization_for_Security_and_Co-operation_in_Europe> deployed a full
monitoring mission for the vote.^[7] <#cite_note-:5-8> With Orbán
seeking a fourth consecutive term in office, preliminary results showed
his party Fidesz <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fidesz> was set to
outperform polls, retain its supermajority and control 135 seats of the
199-seat Parliament, comfortably ahead of the opposition alliance United
for Hungary <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_for_Hungary>, which
was set to win 57 seats after 100% of the votes had been counted.^[8]
<#cite_note-:3-9> The Mi Hazánk <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Our_Homeland_Movement> party won seats for the first time, obtaining 6
seats.^[9] <#cite_note-hungarytoday.hu-10>


      Analysis[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2022_Hungarian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=12>]

Domestically, the degree of Fidesz's victory was attributed to Fidesz's
influence over the domestic media landscape, voters' desire for
stability during the ongoing Russian invasion of Ukraine <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine>, strong ideological
differences within the main opposition coalition, the perception of
Márki-Zay as inexperienced, and urban–rural <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Urban%E2%80%93rural_political_divide> political polarization
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_polarization>.^[31]
<#cite_note-35> Some analysts claimed that the majority of Jobbik
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jobbik> voters turned out for Fidesz or
Mi Hazánk instead of the united opposition.^[32] <#cite_note-36> Márki-
Zay shared this assessment, admitting that the united opposition may
have lost up to "two thirds" of Jobbik voters.^[33] <#cite_note-37>
Other opposition leaders could not immediately agree on how to assess
their defeat. DK leader Ferenc Gyurcsány <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Ferenc_Gyurcs%C3%A1ny> and Jobbik leader Péter Jakab <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P%C3%A9ter_Jakab> blamed Márky-Zay,^[34]
<#cite_note-38> ^[35] <#cite_note-39> ^[36] <#cite_note-40> while
Bernadett Szél <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernadett_Sz%C3%A9l> and
Ákos Hadházy <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%81kos_Hadh%C3%A1zy> did
not.^[37] <#cite_note-41> Péter Ungár <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
P%C3%A9ter_Ung%C3%A1r> claimed that while it was a "strategic mistake"
for a center-left alliance to nominate the conservative Márky-Zay, the
united opposition's "elitist" rhetoric may have hurt it with rural
voters even in traditionally left-leaning constituencies.^[38]
<#cite_note-42>

/Politico <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politico>/ predicted Orbán's
victory would lead to further clashes between the Hungarian government
and European Union institutions.^[39] <#cite_note-43>

Analysts found that Fidesz performed strongest in areas with high
birthrates^[40] <#cite_note-44> and with lower educational or economic
attainment,^[41] <#cite_note-45> ^[42] <#cite_note-46> while the united
opposition did best in Budapest and in a few other urban areas.^[43]
<#cite_note-47>


      Conduct[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2022_Hungarian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=13>]

International observers' assessments of the electoral process were
mixed. The Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_for_Security_and_Co-
operation_in_Europe> (OSCE) monitoring mission evaluated that "the legal
framework forms an adequate basis for democratic elections to be held,
but a number of key aspects fall short of international standards." The
OSCE added that the election day passed peacefully, with observers
assessing the process as well-organized, orderly, and smooth. The
monitoring mission further reported that the elections "offered voters
distinct alternatives and were well run, but, while competitive, was
marred by the pervasive overlapping of government and ruling coalition’s
messaging that blurred the line between state and party, as well as by
media bias and opaque campaign funding."^[44] <#cite_note-48> Human
Rights Watch <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch> stated
that the election was "marred by serious concerns about its fairness,"
adding that the "EU has responded insufficiently to the hollowing out of
democracy and the rule of law in Hungary,"^[45] <#cite_note-49> while
Freedom House <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_House> described
the election as neither free nor fair.^[46] <#cite_note-50>


      Anomalies over postal ballots[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/
      index.php?
      title=2022_Hungarian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=14>]

Since the 2014 parliamentary election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2014_Hungarian_parliamentary_election>, the right to vote is extended to
Hungarian citizens, after a registration process, who do not have a
permanent residence in Hungary (i.e. mostly the Hungarian diaspora
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_diaspora> in the neighboring
countries, who have dual citizenship); however, they can only vote for
the national list of Hungarian parties, so they do not have a chance to
vote in individual constituencies. Unlike those Hungarians who have a
Hungarian address but live abroad, the members of the Hungarian diaspora
can cast their ballots by post without having to travel to an embassy or
consulate. Critics say this method do not provide sufficient guarantees
to prevent election fraud. Since 2014, the ruling party Fidesz–KDNP
usually acquired more than 90 percent of the vote among the Hungarian
diaspora.^[47] <#cite_note-targumures-51>

Transtelex.ro, a Hungarian-language Romanian news portal revealed all
possible frauds allowed by the legally undeveloped system. For instance,
the registration process was not secure, as the data allowed a voter to
register on behalf of another person and retrieve voting documents to
any address. In the case of identification sheets, it was not possible
to determine with absolute certainty whether it had actually been
completed by the person whose name appears on it. As a result of a
change in legislation in 2021, it was possible to return the vote
without the posted security sealed envelope, therefore, there was no
guarantee that the ballot paper had not been replaced.^[48]
<#cite_note-52> There were several reports that a person could also
easily vote on behalf of their family members. Telex.hu <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telex.hu> reported a case that grandparents of a
woman from Odorheiu Secuiesc <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Odorheiu_Secuiesc>, who resided in Budapest in the weeks of the
election, filled her personal data and voted on her behalf. She said, "I
have already been outraged that they have filled in the official paper
and sent it for me, abusing my personal rights, but, in addition, they
have voted for a party that I do not support because it represents an
ideologically opposed position to me".^[49] <#cite_note-53>

According to reports, the activists of the Fidesz-ally Alliance of
Vojvodina Hungarians <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Alliance_of_Vojvodina_Hungarians> (VMSZ) delivered the ballot papers
instead of the Pošta Srbije <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Po%C5%A1ta_Srbije> in Vojvodina <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Vojvodina>, Serbia <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serbia>. News portal /
Szabad Magyar Szó/ reported that the party officials sent messages to
the registered voters to come to the local offices of the VMSZ to cast
their ballot there.^[47] <#cite_note-targumures-51> In Transylvania
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transylvania>, Romania <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romania>, pro-Fidesz organizations and parties of
the Hungarian minority, including the parliamentary party Democratic
Alliance of Hungarians in Romania <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Democratic_Alliance_of_Hungarians_in_Romania> (UDMR or RMDSZ) also urged
the registered voters to pass on their votes to them and not to entrust
the postal service with the delivery of their ballot papers, because
there is less chance that the vote will reach Hungary. In response, the
Poșta Română <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Po%C8%99ta_Rom%C3%A2n%C4%83>
complained against the call.^[47] <#cite_note-targumures-51>

In the area between Livezeni <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livezeni>
and Târgu Mureș <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C3%A2rgu_Mure%C8%99> in
Romania, a bag containing partially burnt Hungarian election postal
ballot papers was found on illegal waste dump on 1 April 2022. Every
ballot paper was filled out with votes for the opposition United for
Hungary alliance or Our Homeland Movement.^[50] <#cite_note-54> The
National Election Office (NVI) and the Hungarian Civil Liberties Union
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_Civil_Liberties_Union> (TASZ)
filed a criminal complaint over the burnt ballot papers to the Hungarian
Police <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rend%C5%91rs%C3%A9g>. Péter Márki-
Zay, prime minister-candidate of the United for Hungary called on the
annulment of all postal votes. Anna Orosz <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Anna_Orosz> of the Momentum Movement demanded that the government
ensure a level playing field for all Hungarian citizens voting abroad.
Jobbik politician Dániel Z. Kárpát <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
D%C3%A1niel_Z._K%C3%A1rp%C3%A1t> compared the scandal with the infamous
"blue-ballot" election in 1947 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
1947_Hungarian_parliamentary_election>. The Fidesz blamed the United for
Hungary for burning the ballot papers, saying "the desperate left-wing
is capable of anything despicable". The Fidesz-ally UDMR also condemned
the "accusations of election fraud", assuming "an organized provocation"
against the ethnic Hungarian minority in Romania.^[47] <#cite_note-
targumures-51> The National Election Commission (NVB) announced on 3
April it will not investigate the issue of the discarded and burned
postal ballots, because the election law only covers the territory of
Hungary. The Curia of Hungary <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Curia_of_Hungary> upheld the NVB's decision on 10 April 2022.^[51]
<#cite_note-55>

The ruling Fidesz acquired 93.91 percent of the mail votes, which
accounted for 8% of all votes cast on the party's national list. This
meant an extra mandate for Fidesz. The United for Hungary and the Our
Homeland gained only 4.1 and 1.06 percent, respectively.^[52]
<#cite_note-56>
