Early parliamentary elections were held in the Slovak Republic <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovakia> on 30 September 2023 to elect members of
the National Council <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
National_Council_(Slovakia)>. Regular elections were scheduled to be
held in 2024. However, on 15 December 2022 the government <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabinet_of_Eduard_Heger> lost a no-confidence
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No-confidence> vote.^[1] <#cite_note-1>
Subsequently, the National Council amended the Constitution so that an
early election could be held on 30 September 2023.^[2] <#cite_note-2>
This was the first snap election in the country since 2012 <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Slovak_parliamentary_election>.

The left-wing populist <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-
wing_populism> and social conservative <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Social_conservatism>, Direction – Social Democracy <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_%E2%80%93_Social_Democracy> (Smer-SD),
led by former Prime Minister <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Prime_Minister_of_Slovakia> Robert Fico <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Robert_Fico>, emerged as the largest party, winning 42 seats. The
social-liberal <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_liberalism> and
pro-European <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pro-Europeanism>,
Progressive Slovakia <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Progressive_Slovakia> (PS) came in second, with 32 seats. Former Prime
Minister Peter Pellegrini <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Peter_Pellegrini>'s social-democratic <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Social_democracy>, Voice – Social Democracy <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Voice_%E2%80%93_Social_Democracy> (Hlas-SD), which split from Smer-
SD in 2020, came in third with 27 seats. The conservative <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservatism>, OĽaNO <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/O%C4%BDaNO> and its allies won 16 seats, less than a quarter of
their total in the 2020 election. The Christian-democratic <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_democracy>, Christian Democratic
Movement <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Movement>
(KDH) and the right-wing populist <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-
wing_populism>, Slovak National Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Slovak_National_Party> (SNS) re-entered the National Council after
failing to achieve the vote threshold in 2020, winning twelve and ten
seats respectively. The classical liberal <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Classical_liberalism>, Freedom and Solidarity <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_and_Solidarity> (SaS) won eleven seats,
tying the 2012 election as their worst result since the party's
founding.^[3] <#cite_note-3>

As no single party or alliance reached the 76 seats needed for a
majority, a coalition government was needed. A coalition government of
Smer-SD <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Direction_%E2%80%93_Social_Democracy>, Hlas-SD <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voice_%E2%80%93_Social_Democracy>, and SNS
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovak_National_Party> was formed. A new
government <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fico%27s_Fourth_Cabinet> with
Robert Fico <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Fico> as prime
minister was sworn in on 25 October 2023.^[4] <#cite_note-
SwearsInAPNews2023-4>


    Background[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Slovak_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=1>]

Prior to the previous election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2020_Slovak_parliamentary_election>, the Slovak Republic <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovakia> experienced a period of political
turbulence, triggered by the murder of investigative journalist Ján
Kuciak and his fiancée Martina Kušnírová in 2018. The incident led to
widespread protests and the resignation of then-Prime Minister Robert
Fico <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Fico>.^[5] <#cite_note-5>

The election itself was won by the movement of Ordinary People and
Independent Personalities <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Ordinary_People_and_Independent_Personalities> (OĽaNO) led by Igor
Matovič <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igor_Matovi%C4%8D>. The party
emerged as the victor, gaining over a quarter of the popular vote, which
translated to 53 seats in the 150-seat National Council. OĽaNO formed a
coalition government <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Matovi%C4%8D_Cabinet> with several other parties, ending the long-
standing dominance of Direction – Social Democracy <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_%E2%80%93_Social_Democracy> (SMER–
SD).^[6] <#cite_note-6>


    Electoral system[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Slovak_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=2>]

The 150 members of the National Council <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
National_Council_(Slovakia)> were elected by proportional representation
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportional_representation> in a single
nationwide constituency with an electoral threshold <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_threshold> of 5% for single parties, 7%
for coalitions of two or three parties, and 10% for coalitions of four
or more parties. The election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election>
used the open list <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_list> system,
with seats allocated using largest remainder method <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Largest_remainder_method> with Hagenbach-Bischoff
quota <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hagenbach-Bischoff_quota>, a
variant of the D'Hondt method <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
D%27Hondt_method>.^[7] <#cite_note-7> Voters were able to cast up to
four preferential votes for candidates of the selected party.^[8]
<#cite_note-8>

All citizens of the Slovak Republic <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Slovakia> were allowed to vote except for citizens under 18 years of
age, felons <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prisoner> in prison convicted
of serious offenses, and people declared ineligible to perform legal
acts by court. Voters abroad on election day were allowed to vote by
mail <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postal_voting>.^[9] <#cite_note-9>
All citizens, who were 21 years of age or older on the election day and
are permanent residents of Slovakia, were allowed to run as candidates
except for prisoners <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prisoner>, convicted
felons, and those declared ineligible to perform legal acts by
court.^[10] <#cite_note-10> All participating parties must register 90
days before election day and pay a deposit <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Election_deposit> of €17,000 which would be refunded to all parties
gaining at least 3% of the votes.

Voters not present in their electoral district at the time of the
elections were allowed to request a voting certificate <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voter_registration> (voličský preukaz), which
allowed them to vote in any district regardless of their residency.
Voters abroad on election day <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Election_day> were allowed to request a postal vote. According to the
Central Election Committee, approximately 72,000 citizens of the Slovak
Republic <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovakia> living abroad had
requested a postal vote for the election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Election>. The deadline for requests passed on 9 August 2023.

    Issues and developments[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Slovak_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=6>]

2023 election issues included high inflation, Slovakia's position on the
war in Ukraine <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine> and the related global energy crisis
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2021%E2%80%932023_global_energy_crisis>,
COVID-19 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19>, internal fights
within the previous government, corruption scandals and
immigration;^[22] <#cite_note-soon_2023_09_28_cnn-31> ^[23] <#cite_note-
knife_edge_2023_09_27_reuters-32> questions of rights and values
(particularly LGBTQ+ <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LGBTQ%2B> issues)
were covered during the campaign by Al Jazeera English <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al_Jazeera_English>, BBC <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC>^[23] <#cite_note-
knife_edge_2023_09_27_reuters-32> ^[24] <#cite_note-
fico_2023_09_29_bbc-33> and Pravda <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Pravda_(Slovakia)>.^[25] <#cite_note-34>

By the week of the election, popularity polls indicated that the two
strongest parties were the Progressive Slovakia <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Progressive_Slovakia> (led by pro-European Michal
Šimečka <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michal_%C5%A0ime%C4%8Dka>, a
member of the European Parliament <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
European_Parliament> (EP) since 2019, and EP Vice-President since 2022,
who has committed to maintaining support for Ukraine) and Smer-SD
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_%E2%80%93_Social_Democracy>
(headed by Robert Fico <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Fico>,
three-time former prime minister, who has committed to ending Slovakia's
support for Ukraine); however, neither appeared to be commanding a
majority, and the BBC <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
British_Broadcasting_Corporation> projected neither would top 20% of the
vote,^[24] <#cite_note-fico_2023_09_29_bbc-33> so each would have to
attempt to build a coalition with other parties to achieve the majority
needed to rule.^[22] <#cite_note-soon_2023_09_28_cnn-31> ^[23]
<#cite_note-knife_edge_2023_09_27_reuters-32> ^[24] <#cite_note-
fico_2023_09_29_bbc-33> Consequently, as many as 10 parties could wind
up in the government.^[24] <#cite_note-fico_2023_09_29_bbc-33>

The third-largest party, which tipped the balance in favor of Robert
Fico <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Fico>, is Hlas–SD <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voice_%E2%80%93_Social_Democracy> (Voice), the
moderate-left party of Peter Pellegrini <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Peter_Pellegrini> (formerly of Smer-SD, and former prime minister, 2018–
20).

    Aftermath[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Slovak_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=9>]

As analysts predicted, Peter Pellegrini <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Peter_Pellegrini>'s Hlas-SD played the role of kingmaker in the
"jockeying" that characterized the election's aftermath.^[26]
<#cite_note-35> Two viable coalitions emerged: one consisting of Fico's
Smer-SD, Hlas-SD, and SNS <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Slovak_National_Party>; the other of Hlas-SD, PS <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Progressive_Slovakia>, KDH <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Movement>, and SaS <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_and_Solidarity>. On 2 October 2023, two
days after the election, president Zuzana Čaputová <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zuzana_%C4%8Caputov%C3%A1> tasked Robert Fico
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Fico>, as the leader of the now-
largest parliamentary party, with forming a government within 14 days.
On 3 October, she held "informal discussions" with PS's leader Michal
Šimečka <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michal_%C5%A0ime%C4%8Dka> about
the possibility of a PS-led coalition before meeting with Pellegrini and
KDH's Milan Majerský. Following this meeting, Peter Pellegrini <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Pellegrini> stated that his party was not
ruling out either coalition.

According to SNS leader Andrej Danko <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Andrej_Danko>, the ensuing negotiations revolved around Peter Pellegrini
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Pellegrini>'s future role. Robert
Fico <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Fico> allegedly offered
Pellegrini the role of Speaker of the National Council <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_(Slovakia)>, while Michal Šimečka
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michal_%C5%A0ime%C4%8Dka> was willing to
support Peter Pellegrini <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Peter_Pellegrini> for Prime Minister in exchange for PS receiving the
Ministry of the Interior.^[27] <#cite_note-36> On 10 October, Hlas-SD
announced that it had rejected PS's offer.^[28] <#cite_note-37> ^[29]
<#cite_note-38> On 11 October, Smer-SD, Hlas-SD, and SNS ratified their
coalition agreement, according to which they were to receive 6, 7, and 3
ministerial portfolios, respectively.^[30] <#cite_note-39> ^[31]
<#cite_note-40> ^[32] <#cite_note-41>

On 12 October, the Party of European Socialists <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Party_of_European_Socialists> (PES) suspended
Smer-SD <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Direction_%E2%80%93_Social_Democracy> and Hlas-SD <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voice_%E2%80%93_Social_Democracy> over their plans
to enter into coalition with SNS, which the PES views as a "radical-
right party."^[33] <#cite_note-42> One week later, on 19 October, Zuzana
Čaputová <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zuzana_%C4%8Caputov%C3%A1>
announced she would not approve the coalition government's nominee for
Minister of Environment, Rudolf Huliak (SNS), due to his avowed climate
change denialism <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_denial>.
This delayed the swearing-in of the new government.^[34] <#cite_note-43>
^[35] <#cite_note-44> ^[36] <#cite_note-45> After the coalition partners
replaced Rudolf Huliak with Tomáš Taraba <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Taraba> (SNS) as their nominee for the post, Zuzana
Čaputová <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zuzana_%C4%8Caputov%C3%A1>
accepted the coalition's government. She swore in the new government
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fico%27s_Fourth_Cabinet>, with Robert
Fico <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Fico> at its helm, on 25
October.^[37] <#cite_note-46> ^[4] <#cite_note-SwearsInAPNews2023-4>
^[38] <#cite_note-47>
