The *2023 Polish parliamentary election* took place on Sunday, 15
October 2023, per the Polish Constitution <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Constitution_of_Poland>. Seats in both the lower house, the Sejm
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sejm>, and the Senate <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senate_of_Poland> were contested. At the polls, a
referendum <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Polish_referendum> -
containing four questions concerning economic and immigration policy of
the government - was also voted on.

At the previous 2019 Polish parliamentary election <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_Polish_parliamentary_election>, the ruling
right-wing Law and Justice Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Law_and_Justice> (PiS) had held onto its majority in the Sejm with Prime
Minister Mateusz Morawiecki <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Mateusz_Morawiecki> forming a second government <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Cabinet_of_Mateusz_Morawiecki>. The
opposition, including the Civic Platform Party <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_Platform> and others, secured a Senate
majority. In the lead-up to the 2023 elections, opposition leader and
former prime minister, Donald Tusk <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Donald_Tusk>, led the Civic Coalition <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Civic_Coalition_(Poland)> political alliance in opposition to the PiS.

The United Right <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Right_(Poland)>
alliance won a plurality of seats but fell short of a Sejm majority. The
opposition, consisting of the Civic Coalition <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Civic_Coalition_(Poland)>, Third Way <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Third_Way_(Poland)>, and The Left <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
The_Left_(Poland)>, achieved a combined total vote of 54%, managing to
form a majority coalition government <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Coalition_government>.^[2] <#cite_note-4> ^[3] <#cite_note-5> In the
Senate, the opposition electoral alliance Senate Pact 2023 <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senate_Pact_2023> won a plurality of the vote and
a majority of seats. Voter turnout was 74.4%, the highest in contested
elections and the highest since the fall of the communist Polish
People's Republic <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Polish_People%27s_Republic>, beating previous records set in 1989 and 2019.


    Background[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Polish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=1>]


      2019 Polish parliamentary election[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/
      w/index.php?
      title=2023_Polish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=2>]

The 2019 parliamentary election saw a record high turnout, with over 60%
of registered electors participating. It also saw the centre-left, in
the form of Lewica <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Left_(Poland)>,
entering the Sejm <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sejm> after four years
on the outside looking in. Conversely, the far right <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far-right_politics_in_Poland> united under the
Konfederacja <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Confederation_Liberty_and_Independence> (Kon) banner to enter one of the
two chambers of parliament for the first time since the 2005
elections.^[4] <#cite_note-BBC-6>

Right-wing <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-wing_politics> parties,
coalesced around the ruling national-conservative <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_conservatism> Law and Justice <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_and_Justice> (PiS) within the United Right
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Right_(Poland)> (ZP) alliance, won
the highest percentage of votes ever received since the complete return
to democracy in 1991 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
1991_Polish_parliamentary_election>, maintaining their majority in the
Sejm, but losing it in the Senate <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Senate_of_Poland>. The PiS party president, Jarosław Kaczyński <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaros%C5%82aw_Kaczy%C5%84ski>, thus saw his
position as the country's strongman strengthened, despite occupying no
governmental position. This result saw the second reelection of a
majority government since the fall of the Eastern Bloc <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Bloc>. Despite not defeating PiS, the main
opposition party, the liberal <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Liberalism_in_Poland> Civic Platform <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Civic_Platform> (PO), itself within the Civic Coalition <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_Coalition_(Poland)> (KO) alliance,
progressed in the senate, though without winning a majority of seats on
its own. The opposition altogether did win a majority of seats in the
senate, thanks to Lewica, the Polish Coalition <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish_Coalition> (PSL) and independent
candidates' gains.^[4] <#cite_note-BBC-6> ^[5] <#cite_note-WaPo-7>

One month after the vote, the incumbent Prime Minister <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Poland>, Mateusz Morawiecki
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mateusz_Morawiecki>, formed his second
government. Its composition showed the so-called "moderate" right
strengthened, which Morawiecki was part of, alongside a weakening of the
"radical" right, led by the Justice minister <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_(Poland)> Zbigniew Ziobro <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zbigniew_Ziobro>. This strategy was mainly to
appeal to the more moderate electorate for the 2020 Polish presidential
election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2020_Polish_presidential_election>.^[6] <#cite_note-8> Morawiecki's
government received the Sejm's confidence on 19 November with 237 votes
for, 214 against and three abstentions.^[7] <#cite_note-9> ^[8]
<#cite_note-10>


      2020 presidential election[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/
      index.php?
      title=2023_Polish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=3>]

The 2020 presidential election saw the reelection of incumbent president
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Poland> Andrzej Duda
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrzej_Duda>, himself a member of Law
and Justice <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_and_Justice>. Originally
planned in May, the elections were very affected by the then ongoing
COVID-19 pandemic <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
COVID-19_pandemic_in_Poland>. The government's proposal to maintain the
election in May but only through postal votes launched a strong polemic,
with the opposition denouncing the unequal campaigning capacities of the
incumbent president compared to other candidates within the context of
the lockdown and quarantine measures. The election was then postponed to
late June following a compromise within the ruling coalition and the
opposition's approval, partly thanks to the latter's control of the
Senate.^[9] <#cite_note-LaCroix-11>

Despite the pandemic, both rounds of voting saw higher turnouts, with
Duda facing the Mayor <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mayor_of_Warsaw> of
Warsaw <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warsaw>, Rafał Trzaskowski
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rafa%C5%82_Trzaskowski>, a member of
Civic Platform <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_Platform>. Duda beat
Trzaskowski, gathering 51% of the votes against the latter's 49%. These
results were the most closely fought presidential elections since the
return of democracy.^[10] <#cite_note-12>

Duda's victory allows PiS take advantage from his presidential veto in
case of an opposition victory in the legislative elections, with the
opposition needing a three-fifths majority, which currently amounts to
276 seats, to override one.^[11] <#cite_note-13>


      2023 Polish protests[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2023_Polish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=4>]

In May 2023, a law previously passed by the Sejm with the votes of the
governing parties came into force, which provides for the establishment
of a commission that can, without a court order, exclude politicians
from public office for a period of ten years if, in their opinion, the
politician was influenced by Russian interests. According to the law,
the commission must examine whether this applies to Polish government
politicians from 2007 (after PiS' defeat in the 2007 election <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2007_Polish_parliamentary_election>). According to
critics, the law could have been used as an instrument to prohibit
selected opposition politicians from taking part in the parliamentary
elections.^[12] <#cite_note-14> Polish media therefore spoke of a "Lex
Tusk" – a law aimed at the opposition leader and former prime minister
Donald Tusk <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Tusk> (2007–2014), who
could have been excluded from the parliamentary elections in October
2023 as the potentially most promising opposition candidate.^[13]
<#cite_note-15> PiS party circles repeatedly accused Tusk of making
Poland dependent on energy imports from Russia during his term as head
of government. The law drew strong criticism from the United States and
the European Union, which expressed concern that the law jeopardized
freedom and fairness in elections in Poland. President Duda then
softened the law by introducing an amendment to the Sejm on 2 June 2023,
which deprived the proposed commission of the previously planned right
to impose a ban on political activity.^[14] <#cite_note-16>

On 4 June 2023 (the anniversary of the first partially free elections in
Poland in 1989 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
1989_Polish_parliamentary_election>) according to organizers, citing the
city administration, half a million people took part in a "Great March
for Democracy" organized by Tusk's Civic Platform in Warsaw to protest
against the law.^[15] <#cite_note-17> There were also protests with tens
of thousands of participants in other cities, including Krakow, Szczecin
and Częstochowa. The demonstration in Warsaw was joined by numerous
civil rights movements, the Civic Platform spoke of the largest
demonstration in Poland's history since the fall of communism in 1989.
The protest march through the center of Warsaw was also led by the
former Polish President Lech Wałęsa <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Lech_Wa%C5%82%C4%99sa>.^[16] <#cite_note-18> ^[17] <#cite_note-19>


    Electoral system[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Polish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=5>]

The President of Poland <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
President_of_Poland> set the election day to be Sunday, 15 October
2023.^[18] <#cite_note-20> This date was consistent with requirements
posed in Article 98 Section 2 of the Polish Constitution <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Poland>, whereby the election is
to take place within the final 30 days of the current term of Parliament
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
9th_term_Sejm_and_10th_term_Senate_of_Poland> (ending 11 November 2023).
The vote ought to be held on a non-working day – a Sunday or a public
holiday <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Poland>. Other
possible dates included 22 October 29 October, 1 November <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_Saints%27_Day#Poland>, 5 November and 11
November <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
National_Independence_Day_(Poland)>.^[19] <#cite_note-21>

The process of election for the Sejm is through party-list proportional
representation <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Party-
list_proportional_representation> via the D'Hondt method <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Hondt_method> in multi-seat constituencies
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constituency>,^[20] <#cite_note-
electionresources-22> with a 5% threshold for single party (KW) and
citizen committees (KWW) and an 8% threshold for coalitions (KKW).
National minority committees, such as the German minority <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_minority_in_Poland>, can apply to be exempt
from the nation-wide threshold, and in such case participate in the
d'Hondt seat distribution within their constituency, in this specific
case Opole <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
21st_electoral_district_for_the_Sejm_of_the_Republic_of_Poland>,
regardless of the national share of votes.^[21] <#cite_note-23> Contrary
to popular belief, minority committees are /not/ guaranteed seats in the
parliament.^[22] <#cite_note-24>

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Senat_RP_okr%C4%99gi_blank_map.svg>
The 100 Senate constituencies

Senators <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senate_of_Poland> are elected by
first-past-the-post method <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-past-
the-post_voting> in 100 constituencies <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
List_of_constituencies_for_the_Senate_of_the_Republic_of_Poland>. Most
of the opposition (Civic Coalition, New Left and Third Way) signed a so-
called /senate pact <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senate_Pact_2023>/,
under which the parties agreed to enter one commonly accepted candidate
in each district.^[23] <#cite_note-25> This strategy has previously
granted them 51 seats despite losing the Sejm.^[24] <#cite_note-26>

Within the stipulated deadline for submitting electoral committees, 94
committees were applied for registration, of which 85 were registered:
two coalitions, 40 political parties and 43 voters. 46 committees

Sejm and 36 only for the Senate.^[32] <#cite_note-37> ^[33] <#cite_note-38>

      Sejm[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2023_Polish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=16>]

PiS remained the largest party in the Sejm, but with about 35% of the
vote, lost its majority and will most likely be unable to form a
government. The three main opposition groups, Civic Coalition, Third Way
and New Left, took 54% of the votes, winning enough seats to allow them
to take power.^[35] <#cite_note-AP-42> According to the final vote count
by the National Electoral Commission <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
National_Electoral_Commission_(Poland)>, Law and Justice won 194 seats,
the Civic Coalition 157, the Third Way 65, The Left 26, and the
Confederation Liberty and Independence 18.^[36] <#cite_note-43>

Although the German Minority Electoral Committee <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Minority_Electoral_Committee> did win 5.37%
of the vote in the Opole region in this election, they came 6th instead
of the expected 5th place, falling 5,372 votes short. The Opole
Voivodeship represents a total of 12 Sejm seats, and as the 5th place
was taken by the far-right Confederation Liberty and Independence
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confederation_Liberty_and_Independence>,
the last 12th seat, which had previously been won by German Minority,
went to them instead.^[37] <#cite_note-44> As a result, the German
Minority Electoral Committee failed to win any parliamentary seat for
the first time in 32 years.^[38] <#cite_note-45>

    Analysis[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Polish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=21>]

Turnout was 74.7% among women and 73.1% among men, with both giving
similar levels of support for the government and two leading opposition
parties, Civic Coalition and Third Way.^[40] <#cite_note-57> ^[41]
<#cite_note-58> Analysts identified a "youthquake" in which voting by
Poland's young voters had a disproportionate impact on the election
outcome. Turnout for ages 18–29 reached 68.8%, compared to 46.4% in the
previous elections of 2019; among these voters, support for the ruling
party fell to 14.9% from 26.3% four years earlier.^[42] <#cite_note-59>


    Aftermath[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Polish_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=22>]

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Umowa_Koalicyjna.jpg>
Leaders of the three opposition parties after signing the coalition
agreement.

President Andrzej Duda <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrzej_Duda>
later announced that he would hold consultations separately with every
parliamentary party leader on 24 and 25 October.^[43] <#cite_note-60> On
24 October, leaders of the Civic Coalition <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Civic_Coalition_(Poland)>, the Polish People's Party <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish_People%27s_Party>, Poland 2050 <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland_2050>, and The Left <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Left_(Poland)> stated they are ready to form a
government with Donald Tusk as their candidate for prime minister.^[44]
<#cite_note-61> However, Duda had a maximum of 30 days to call
parliament into session, especially if he wanted the ruling Law and
Justice party to try to build a government. Opposition parties had
called on Duda to allow them to form a government as soon as possible
and respect the will of the voters. Representatives of Duda stated that
he would do so within the timeframe that the Constitution demands and
allows.^[45] <#cite_note-62>

On 6 November, Duda named Law and Justice's incumbent prime minister
Mateusz Morawiecki <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mateusz_Morawiecki> as
his prime ministerial nominee.^[46] <#cite_note-63> This move was
criticized by the opposition, as the United Right was 40 seats short of
a majority and no other bloc had agreed to join them for coalition
talks.^[47] <#cite_note-64>

On 10 November, Civic Coalition, Poland 2050, Polish People's Party and
New Left signed a coalition agreement with Tusk as their candidate for
prime minister. The opposition parties wanted to sign the agreement
before the Sejm's first sitting in order to show that they stood ready
to govern. If Morawiecki couldn't secure the Sejm's confidence <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_of_no_confidence> within two weeks of being
sworn in, the Sejm would then designate its own candidate for prime
minister, which is then supposed to be officially appointed by Duda
before 11 December. Most commentators expected Morawiecki to come up
short of the support needed to govern, as no other party willing to go
into coalition with PiS would give it enough support to command the
confidence of the Sejm.^[48] <#cite_note-65>

On 13 November, the newly elected Sejm held its first session. Szymon
Hołownia <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Szymon_Ho%C5%82ownia>, leader of
Poland 2050, was elected Marshal of the Sejm <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Marshal_of_the_Sejm>, winning over the incumbent Elżbieta Witek
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El%C5%BCbieta_Witek> of PiS.^[49]
<#cite_note-66> Later that day, on the first meeting of the Senate,
former Marshal of the Sejm Małgorzata Kidawa-Błońska <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ma%C5%82gorzata_Kidawa-B%C5%82o%C5%84ska> of Civic
Coalition was elected Marshal of the Senate <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Marshal_of_the_Senate_of_the_Republic_of_Poland>.^[50] <#cite_note-67>

On 27 November, Mateusz Morawiecki was sworn in by President Duda for an
unprecedented third term <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Third_Cabinet_of_Mateusz_Morawiecki> as Prime Minister <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Poland>.^[51] <#cite_note-68>
His cabinet <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Third_Cabinet_of_Mateusz_Morawiecki> had been mockingly dubbed the "Two
Weeks Government" by Polish media due to its low likelihood of passing
the confidence vote.^[52] <#cite_note-69> ^[53] <#cite_note-70> ^[54]
<#cite_note-71> Though the far-right Confederation Liberty and
Independence <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Confederation_Liberty_and_Independence> had been suggested by
commentators as a potential coalition partner, Krzysztof Bosak <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krzysztof_Bosak>, leader of the Confederation
component National Movement <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
National_Movement_(Poland)>, told Politico Europe <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politico_Europe> that "there is no chance" of
Confederation supporting a PiS government. Even had Confederation
supported PiS, the United Right would have still been well short of a
majority in the Sejm. Former prime minister Leszek Miller <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leszek_Miller> joked that the Morawiecki
government would not survive even as long as a house fly <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_fly>, saying on Twitter <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twitter> that "Morawiecki's government will not
even have time to pupate <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pupa>, let alone
lay eggs."^[55] <#cite_note-72>

On 11 December, Mateusz Morawiecki's caretaker cabinet lost a vote of
confidence in the Sejm by 190 votes to 266.^[56] <#cite_note-73> ^[57]
<#cite_note-74> Later that day, the Sejm nominated Donald Tusk for prime
minister, who was subsequently confirmed by 248 votes in favour and 201
against.^[58] <#cite_note-75> Tusk's cabinet <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Third_Cabinet_of_Donald_Tusk> was sworn in on 13 December.^[59]
<#cite_note-76>
