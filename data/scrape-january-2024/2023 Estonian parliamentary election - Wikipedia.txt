Parliamentary elections were held in Estonia <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Estonia> on 5 March 2023 to elect all 101 members of the Riigikogu
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riigikogu>. The officially published
election data indicate the victory of the Reform Party <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estonian_Reform_Party>, which won 37 seats in
total, while the Conservative People's Party of Estonia <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_People%27s_Party_of_Estonia> (EKRE)
placed second with 17 seats. The Centre Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Estonian_Centre_Party> won 16 seats, a loss of 10, while Estonia
200 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estonia_200> won 14 seats, gaining
representation in the Riigikogu.

After the previous parliamentary election in 2019 <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_Estonian_parliamentary_election>, the Centre
Party, led by Jüri Ratas <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
J%C3%BCri_Ratas>, formed a government with Ratas serving as prime
minister. His government was brought down in January 2021 after a
corruption investigation, and Kaja Kallas <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Kaja_Kallas> of the Reform Party formed a coalition government
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaja_Kallas%27_first_cabinet> with the
Centre Party, which collapsed in June 2022. Kallas then formed a
government <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaja_Kallas%27_second_cabinet>
with Isamaa <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isamaa> and the Social
Democratic Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Social_Democratic_Party_(Estonia)> and remained in the position of prime
minister.

In January 2023, the National Electoral Committee announced that nine
political parties and ten individual candidates had registered to take
part in the 2023 parliamentary election. During the campaign period,
issues discussed most extensively regarded the Estonian economy, and the
country's national defence and security due to the current Russian
invasion of Ukraine <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine>. Individuals from contesting political
parties also participated in multiple organised debates in January and
February 2023. Voting at foreign embassies for Estonians outside the
country took place from 18 to 23 February, while Estonian residents
could vote during the pre-election period from 27 February to 4 March.

These were the first national elections where more than half of the
votes were cast electronically <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Electronic_voting_in_Estonia> over the Internet. Following the election,
EKRE submitted an appeal to the Supreme Court of Estonia <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Estonia>, requesting that the
results be annulled and claiming that "anomalies and technical errors in
the e-voting process had been observed", though the appeal was later
dismissed. Reform successfully negotiated afterward with the Social
Democratic Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Social_Democratic_Party_(Estonia)> and Estonia 200, forming a government
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaja_Kallas%27_third_cabinet> headed by
Kallas in April.


    Background[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Estonian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=1>]

The previous parliamentary election <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2019_Estonian_parliamentary_election>, which was held in March 2019, saw
the loss of the absolute majority <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Absolute_majority> held by Jüri Ratas's first cabinet <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%BCri_Ratas%27_first_cabinet> in the Riigikogu
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riigikogu>, the unicameral parliament of
Estonia. Ratas's Centre Party, Isamaa <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Isamaa>, and Social Democratic Party <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Social_Democratic_Party_(Estonia)> (SDE) all suffered a setback in
favour of the Reform Party, led by Kallas, and the EKRE.^[1]
<#cite_note-1> Kersti Kaljulaid <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Kersti_Kaljulaid>, then-president of Estonia <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/President_of_Estonia>, gave a mandate to Kallas to form a
government after the election.^[2] <#cite_note-2> The Reform Party
negotiated with the Centre Party, Isamaa, and SDE but ultimately failed
to form a government.^[3] <#cite_note-3> ^[4] <#cite_note-4> ^[5]
<#cite_note-5> After the vote in April 2019, Ratas received the mandate
and successfully formed a government with Isamaa and EKRE.^[6]
<#cite_note-6> Jüri Ratas's second cabinet <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/J%C3%BCri_Ratas%27_second_cabinet> was sworn in on 29 April
2019.^[7] <#cite_note-7> ^[8] <#cite_note-8>

In January 2021, the Centre Party-led government collapsed after a
corruption investigation in which the Centre Party was accused of
requesting financial support of up to €1 million within a year in return
of the €39 million loan to Hillar Teder <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Hillar_Teder>'s real estate development in Tallinn <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tallinn>.^[9] <#cite_note-9> In response, Ratas
resigned as prime minister of Estonia <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Prime_minister_of_Estonia>, while Kallas was invited to form a
government.^[10] <#cite_note-10> She struck a deal with the Centre
Party, with Kallas now serving as prime minister.^[11] <#cite_note-11>
In June 2022, however, the coalition government between the Centre and
Reform Party collapsed due to the Centre Party's opposition to a law on
education.^[12] <#cite_note-:0-12> This occurred during the 2022 Russian
invasion of Ukraine <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine>; the Centre Party was seen as close to
Russians in Estonia <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Russians_in_Estonia>.^[12] <#cite_note-:0-12> In response, Kallas opened
Triple Alliance <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Triple_Alliance_(Estonia)> negotiations with Isamaa and SDE,
successfully forming Kaja Kallas's second cabinet <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaja_Kallas%27_second_cabinet> on 15 July
2022.^[13] <#cite_note-13> ^[14] <#cite_note-14>


    Electoral system[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Estonian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=2>]

The Riigikogu is made up of 101 seats and its representatives are
elected by proportional representation <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Proportional_representation> in twelve multi-member constituencies.^[15]
<#cite_note-15> First, seats are to be filled in 12 constituencies of 5
to 16 seats depending on their population, and the remaining seats,
known as "compensation seats", are allocated using the d'Hondt method
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Hondt_method> to all parties that
exceeded the 5% electoral threshold <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Electoral_threshold>, to bring the results in terms of seats as close as
possible to those of the vote of the population.^[16] <#cite_note-:1-16>
^[17] <#cite_note-:2-17> In 2023, 75 seats were filled in the
constituencies and the remaining 26 were allocated using the d'Hondt
method. Voters have the possibility of casting a preferential vote for
one of the candidates on the list for which they are voting.^[16]
<#cite_note-:1-16> ^[17] <#cite_note-:2-17> If a candidate collects more
preferential votes than the amount of the simple quotient in his
constituency, they are declared elected even if the list for which they
are candidate for fails to cross the 5% electoral threshold.^[16]
<#cite_note-:1-16> ^[17] <#cite_note-:2-17>

In November 2022, Alar Karis <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alar_Karis>,
the president of Estonia <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
President_of_Estonia>, signed the election decree, setting the date for
5 March 2023.^[18] <#cite_note-18> Estonian citizens who are at least 18
years old, are registered in the voting list, have not been declared
incapacitated or convicted of a crime by a court, or are serving a
prison sentence have the right to vote.^[19] <#cite_note-:3-19> ^[20]
<#cite_note-20> In foreign embassies abroad, voting took place from 18
to 23 February 2023, while to vote by mail in foreign countries, the
voter had to submit a written request to the foreign mission by 3
February 2023.^[19] <#cite_note-:3-19> ^[21] <#cite_note-:8-21> Advance
voting in Estonia took place from 27 February to 4 March, meaning that a
voter could vote outside the electoral district of their residence and
electronically.^[19] <#cite_note-:3-19> ^[21] <#cite_note-:8-21> Unlike
in previous elections, on 5 March voters were able to vote in any
precinct within the constituency, while voters who had voted
electronically during the advance voting were able to vote on a paper
ballot, although their electronic vote would be cancelled.^[19]
<#cite_note-:3-19> ^[21] <#cite_note-:8-21> Citizens who were not able
to go to the polling stations or to vote electronically could order a
ballot box at home and vote between 3 and 5 March 2023.^[19]
<#cite_note-:3-19> ^[21] <#cite_note-:8-21> Around eighty foreign
observers monitored the election.^[22] <#cite_note-22>

      Issues[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2023_Estonian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=7>]

The /Eesti Rahvusringhääling <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Eesti_Rahvusringh%C3%A4%C3%A4ling>/ and NPR <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/NPR> commented that the issues that were discussed during the
campaign period were the cost of living crisis, especially regarding
rising prices and inflation, as well as issues regarding security and
national defence due to the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine>.^[31]
<#cite_note-32> ^[32] <#cite_note-33> /Eesti Rahvusringhääling/ also
asserted that the programmes of the contesting parties were largely
influenced by the current war in Ukraine.^[33] <#cite_note-34> Ellu
Saar, a sociology professor, and Triin Lauri, an associate professor at
Tallinn University <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tallinn_University>,
said that in the programmes of EKRE and Isamaa stated their support to
protect the Estonian language <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Estonian_language> in higher education, while Estonia 200 <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estonia_200> and SDE mention united schools where
native Estonian-speaking and Russian-speaking students would study
together.^[34] <#cite_note-:5-35>

Saar and Lauri commented that the Centre Party had a vague opinion
regarding the issue of education.^[34] <#cite_note-:5-35> The Estonian
Greens <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estonian_Greens> and SDE also
mentioned school culture, quality of management, reducing path
dependence as issues they would tackle.^[34] <#cite_note-:5-35> The
Reform Party also stated its support for introducing national defence
classes in schools, while Isamaa said that there should be more
investments in national defence.^[35] <#cite_note-:6-36> The Centre
Party called for higher salaries for those who work to contribute to the
national defence, SDE suggested to implement a new tax regarding defence
spending, while EKRE said that militarised border guards should be
brought back.^[35] <#cite_note-:6-36> Anne Raiste of /Eesti
Rahvusringhääling/ said that the contesting parties all shared similar
positions regarding foreign policy and that they pledged to implement
forms of direct democracy <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Direct_democracy> or a more efficient representative democracy <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_democracy>.^[36] <#cite_note-37>
^[37] <#cite_note-38> In regards to the 2022–2023 Ukrainian refugee
crisis <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
2022%E2%80%932023_Ukrainian_refugee_crisis>, EKRE and Isamaa pledged to
not accept any new refugees <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Refugee>,^[38] <#cite_note-:9-39> ^[39] <#cite_note-:10-40> while the
Reform Party stated that it would continue accepting new Ukrainian
refugees.^[40] <#cite_note-:11-41> Euronews <https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Euronews> said that EKRE's support in opinion polls had fallen due
to alleged links with Russia.^[39] <#cite_note-:10-40>

Regarding the economy, Raiste said that the contesting parties had
pledged to raise pensions to €1,000 or more.^[41] <#cite_note-42> The
Centre Party stated its support for the introduction of progressive
taxation <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Progressive_taxation>, while SDE
and the Greens had adopted similar positions.^[42] <#cite_note-:7-43> On
the other hand, Reform stated that it would balance the budget <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balance_the_budget>, while EKRE called for
reduction of taxes.^[42] <#cite_note-:7-43> Estonia 200 said that labour
taxes should be lowered, while Parempoolsed promised to not introduce
any new taxes.^[42] <#cite_note-:7-43> Scholars Liili Abuladze and Luule
Sakkeus stated that EKRE and Isamaa put their family policy focuses on
families with many children and that the needs of those most vulnerable
are hardly addressed.^[43] <#cite_note-44> EKRE stated its opposition to
transition to sustainable energy <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Transition_to_sustainable_energy> to lower energy bills, while the
Reform Party pledged to adopt sustainable energy.^[38] <#cite_note-:
9-39> ^[40] <#cite_note-:11-41> EKRE also criticised Kallas's Reform
Party due to the growing inflation.^[44] <#cite_note-45> The Centre
Party campaigned on infrastructure investments and affordable
housing.^[45] <#cite_note-:15-46>

Election results by municipalities

Voting at Estonian embassies abroad took place between 18 and 23
February 2023.^[65] <#cite_note-:4-66> The National Electoral Committee
had announced that 47.3% of voters cast their ballot during the pre-
election period, which began on 27 February and ended before 5 March, a
majority of whom voted online.^[65] <#cite_note-:4-66> ^[66]
<#cite_note-67> ^[67] <#cite_note-68> Voting stations on 5 March 2023
were opened from 9:00 am to 8:00 pm.^[68] <#cite_note-69> ^[69]
<#cite_note-70> There were 966,129 citizens that had the right to vote
in the election.^[70] <#cite_note-71>

The turnout progressed towards 63% by 7:00 pm according to /Eesti
Rahvusringhääling/, with the highest turnout being in the Rapla County
and the lowest in the Ida-Viru County.^[71] <#cite_note-72> With 312,181
electronic votes, these were the first elections where more than half of
votes were cast online.^[72] <#cite_note-73> Later that day, /Eesti
Rahvusringhääling/, Reuters <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuters>, and
the Associated Press <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_Press>
reported that the Reform Party won the most votes and seats in the
Riigikogu, with EKRE placing second and the Centre Party placing
third.^[38] <#cite_note-:9-39> ^[73] <#cite_note-:13-74> ^[74]
<#cite_note-75> Additionally, /Eesti Rahvusringhääling/ reported that
Estonia 200 would enter the Riigikogu for the first time since its
formation.^[73] <#cite_note-:13-74> ^[75] <#cite_note-:14-76>

    Aftermath[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
    title=2023_Estonian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=12>]

Tanel Kiik <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanel_Kiik>, the vice-chair of
the Centre Party, had said that "the Centre Party would be satisfied if
it were to get 20 seats" in the Riigikogu, while Sven Mikser <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sven_Mikser>, the former chairman of the SDE, said
that "nine mandates is probably the best the party can do in Estonia
today".^[78] <#cite_note-:12-79> Ratas later described the results as
"mediocre".^[78] <#cite_note-:12-79> After the publication of e-vote
results, Kallas gave a speech and thanked her voters.^[78] <#cite_note-:
12-79> ^[79] <#cite_note-:16-80> Kallas was congratulated by Sanna Marin
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanna_Marin>, the prime minister of
Finland, and Krišjānis Kariņš <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Kri%C5%A1j%C4%81nis_Kari%C5%86%C5%A1>, the prime minister of
Latvia.^[80] <#cite_note-81> ^[81] <#cite_note-82>

Martin Helme <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Helme>, the leader of
EKRE, stated that "he does not trust the e-vote results" and demanded a
recount.^[78] <#cite_note-:12-79> ^[79] <#cite_note-:16-80> On 9 March
2023, EKRE made an appeal to the Supreme Court of Estonia <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Estonia>, requesting the results
to be annulled and claiming that "several anomalies and technical errors
in the e-voting process had been observed".^[82] <#cite_note-83> Lawyer
Paul Keres of EKRE also claimed that the e-voting process is
unconstitutional.^[83] <#cite_note-84> This case was dismissed on 13
March.^[84] <#cite_note-85>


      Government formation[edit <https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?
      title=2023_Estonian_parliamentary_election&action=edit&section=13>]

As the party that won the most seats in the election, Reform Party began
negotiations between the parties to attempt to form a government.^[75]
<#cite_note-:14-76> /Eesti Rahvusringhääling/ reported that while
experts predicted that the Reform–Estonia 200–SDE coalition would be
formed after the election, Kallas only ruled out a coalition with
EKRE.^[45] <#cite_note-:15-46> ^[78] <#cite_note-:12-79> ^[85]
<#cite_note-86> A day after the election, Kallas said "that it is clear
that voters expect to see Estonia 200 in the government", while she also
said that there are four possible coalitions for the Reform Party to
form.^[86] <#cite_note-87> ^[87] <#cite_note-88> Kallas invited Estonia
200 and SDE for government talks on 7 March 2023.^[88] <#cite_note-89>
Lauri Läänemets <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Lauri_L%C3%A4%C3%A4nemets>, the leader of SDE, confirmed on 7 April that
the Reform Party, Estonia 200, and SDE reached an agreement.^[89]
<#cite_note-90> A day later, Kallas revealed the composition of her new
cabinet.^[90] <#cite_note-91> ^[91] <#cite_note-92> The coalition
agreement was signed on 10 April.^[92] <#cite_note-93> ^[93]
<#cite_note-94> ^[94] <#cite_note-95> Soon after, Kallas became the
prime-minister designate,^[95] <#cite_note-96> with the Riigikogu
approving the coalition government on 12 April.^[96] <#cite_note-:17-97>
^[97] <#cite_note-98> Her cabinet <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/
Kaja_Kallas%27_third_cabinet> was sworn in on 17 April.^[96]
<#cite_note-:17-97>
